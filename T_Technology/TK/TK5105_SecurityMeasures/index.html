Online Privacy Protection

Browser Extensions and Online Tools

  • Privacy Badger: a browser extension developed by the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF).
  • NoScript: browser extension for FireFox and SeaMonkey that disables JavaScript and allows you to selectively enable scripts on a script-by-script and site-by-site basis. This is very effective but requires more work by the user.
  • Tor Project: Tor is free software and an open network that helps you defend against traffic analysis, a form of network surveillance that threatens personal freedom and privacy, confidential business activities and relationships, and state security.
  • Panopticlick: online tool by the EFF that can analyze how well your browser and add-ons protect you against online tracking techniques.
  • BrowserSpy: a site where you can see just how much information your browser reveals about you and your system.

Note: Don't rely on Virtual Private Networks (VPN) alone to provide anonymity and privacy. A VPN provider still knows your IP address, who you are and what non-encrypted data you send over the internet. Websites can still track you unles you take additional measures (i.e. blocking cookies and trackers). In addition, you should not trust VPN providers based in the Five Eyes countries, i.e., the USA, the UK, Canada, Australia and New Zealand.

Secure E-Mail

This section is not about keeping your e-mail message safe from prying eyes but about reducing security risks related to reading e-mail messages and attachments.

  • How to inspect an email attachment without downloading it? A question posted on Security Stack Exchange in September 2018.
  • Tracy, Miles; Jansen, Wayne; Scarfone, Karen; Butterfield, Jason: SP 800-45 Version 2: Guidelines on Electronic Mail Security, Computer Security Resource Centre (CSRC) at National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), USA, February 2007. The document's abstract says:
    This document was developed in furtherance of NIST's statutory responsibilities under the Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA) of 2002, Public Law 107-347. The purpose of the publication is to recommend security practices for designing, implementing, and operating email systems on public and private networks. It contains information on popular email encryption standards and other standards relating to email. It presents general information on securing mail servers' operating systems and specific guidance on securing mail server applications, protecting messages traversing servers, and securing access to mailboxes. It also provides information regarding email client security and mail server administration.
    Most of this document is aimed at people administering mail servers, but chapter 8.5 contains a checklist for securing mail clients. The checklist contains recommendations such as the following:
    • Disable automatic message preview
    • Disable automatic opening of messages
    • Disable automatic loading of pictures in messages
    • Disable downloading and processing of active content (if appropriate)
    • Enable anti-spam and anti-phishing features
    • Disable ability of mail client to store username and passwords
    • Enable and install only absolutely necessary plug-ins from trusted sources
  • Department of Defence, Australian Government: 2017 Australian Government Information Security Manual Controls, Australian Cyber Security Centre (ACSC), Australian Government, 29 September 2017. Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 Australia licence. Pages 182 — 191 are about e-mail security.

Data Erasure

  • BleachBit: an open-source disk cleaning program for Linux and Windows.

Background information:

  • Gutmann method (Wikipedia): an algorithm for securely erasing the contents of computer hard disk drives, such as files.
  • Data remanence (Wikipedia): the residual representation of digital data that remains even after attempts have been made to remove or erase the data.
  • Data erasure (Wikipedia): a software-based method of overwriting the data that aims to completely destroy all electronic data residing on a hard disk drive or other digital media by using zeros and ones to overwrite data onto all sectors of the device.
  • Collins, Barry: The hard disk that survived six months in the sea, Alphr, 07.12.2009.
    According to this article, a data recovery company managed to recover several gigabytes of data from a hard disk that had spent six momnths on the seabed. This is why more reliable methods are needed to reliably wipe data (for example, using DBAN and then physically destroying the drive with a sledge hammer).
  • Can magnets damage or wipe a laptop hard drive? According to the answers to this question, strong magnets can at best damage a hard disk but they cannot wipe data.

    Dozens of companies use smartphone locations to help advertisers and even hedge funds. They say it’s anonymous, but the data shows how personal it is. At least 75 companies receive anonymous, precise location data from apps whose users enable location services to get local news and weather or other information, The Times found. Several of those businesses claim to track up to 200 million mobile devices in the United States — about half those in use last year. The database reviewed by The Times — a sample of information gathered in 2017 and held by one company — reveals people’s travels in startling detail, accurate to within a few yards and in some cases updated more than 14,000 times a day. But those with access to the raw data — including employees or clients — could still identify a person without consent. They could follow someone they knew, by pinpointing a phone that regularly spent time at that person’s home address. Or, working in reverse, they could attach a name to an anonymous dot, by seeing where the device spent nights and using public records to figure out who lived there.
