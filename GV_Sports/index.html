    See also the YouTube channel HITuni - The High Intensity Training University, for example the video Doug McGuff, MD on The Best Routine, Frequency of Training and Recovery for the Over-40s | HITuni (5 minutes, 21.05.2018). This is about exercises that are chosen to that most of the concentration is focused on effort instead of co-ordination. This results in the following types of exercises: (1) an upper-body push in the horizontal plane, (2) an upper-body pull in the horizontal plane, (3) an upper-body push in the vertical plane, (4) an upper-body pull in the vertical plane and (5) a pressing movement with the lower body. You may also want a hinging movement around the hips, e.g. some sort of lumbar extension or a Roman chair extension, superman on the floor or even a deadlift.
    These big body movements will cover most of the musculature throughout the body. In the beginning, you can start Monday-Wednesday-Friday and after a few days move to a Monday-Thursday frequency. If you are feeling fatigued, you can change that to Monday-Friday-Wednesday. Improvements don't necessarily follow the pattern workout-recovery-improvement; sometimes it follows the pattern of a few workout-recovery cycles before improvement can be seen. In one of the comments, HiTuni / Doug McGuff explains what the big five exercises are:
    • a lower body push, e.g. leg press or squat,
    • an upper body horizontal pull, e.g. a multi-joint row,
    • an upper body horizontal push, e.g. a chest press or bench press,
    • an upper body vertical pull, e.g. a pulldown or chin-up.
    Doug McGuff also answers a number of questions in the comments:
    I always hear people talk about protein synthesis and that after 48 hours your muscles stop growing and you should hit them again. But in my opinion your muscles don't start growing until their repaired.
    Muscles also don't suddenly start atrophying just because protein synthesis has returned to baseline. Just looking at protein synthesis is too narrow of a perspective.
    (…) I will do a HIT routine, train to failure on all major muscle groups, but I am putting on top of that some follow up exercise because I worry that maybe I didn't really train to failure.     2x HIT workouts per week is enough resistance training, adding more will likely hinder rather than help results- and can lead to that wiped out feeling you describe. Focus on getting good at going to momentary muscular failure safely and you will have done what needs to be done!
    What other workouts is he doing besides Big 5?? I can’t find anything anywhere on cardio recommendations. Is he walking? Jogging?
    As Childish Gamebino mentioned the "cardio" is in the workout. The metabolic impact of performing sets to momentary failure is akin to sprinting. Being physically active, walking etc. between HIT workouts is a good thing, but there is no need for a formal schedule- just be spontaneously active!

Training advice:

  • Suggestions For Lifting Beginners (Every Damn Day Fitness on YouTube, 6 minutes, 09.05.2018).
    Alan Roberts sells several training programmes on his site, for example E.D.D.F. LEAN MASS HYPERTROPHY PROGRAM and E.D.D.F. VOLUME TRAINING. In this no-nonsense video, he explains that he once considered creating a programme for beginners and why he decided not to do that after all. Beginners should learn the four basic lifts (the bench press, the squat, the deadlift and the overhead press), but these exercises shouldn't be taught using distance learning. The reason is that there are both mental and physical variables that need to be taken into account by a coach who is physically present can correct (and a video can't). Physiological variablese that need to be taken into account include height, weight, age, body fat percentage, the length of the femur, even the length of the femur's neck.
    For this reason, Alan Roberts recommends that beginners should find a strength coach who can teach the four basic lifts in person. After about six months of getting proficient at these exercises, beginners can start worrying about curls or a novice programme, i.e. a three-times-per-week full-body programme that builds on the four basic lifts. Males who have between 12 and 20% body fat shouldn't worry too much about nutrition as long as they stay withing that range. If beginners start aiming for caloric deficits in order to make their abs visible, they will rob themselves of what Alan Roberts calls "noob gains" or lean mass gains. As long as you eat healthy (with sufficient protein, i.e. 1 gram of protein per pound of lean body mass) and work out hard using the four basic lifts, you will develop a good-looking physique. Alan Robets doesn't believe in supplemenation for beginners; they should be getting enough protein through regular food. He stresses the fact that training needs to be consistent: fitness is a lifestyle.
  • Age, Injuries, and Foolish Training Practices | Fitness Is a Marathon Not a Sprint (Every Damn Day Fitness on YouTube, 6 minutes, 02.02.2019).

  • Schoenfeld, Brad; Kolber, Morey; Haimes, Jonathan: The Upright Row: Implications for Preventing Subacromial Impingement, Strength and Conditioning Journal, Volume 33, Issue 5 (October 2011): 25–28.
    In the traditional upright row, the elbows end up above the shoulders and the wrists, which requires internal rotation in the shoulders. The authors propose modifications to the technique to make it safe and effective. The bar should be raised through the elbows, not the wrists, and should move as close to the body as possible. In addition, the arms should be raised so they make an angle of at most 90° with the upper body. The authors also make recommendations regarding training volume, rest periods and weight.
  • Chris Hemsworth's Workout Explained By His Personal Trainer | Train Like a Celebrity (Men's Health on YouTube, 6 minutes, 27.11.2018).
    Luke Zocchi, Chris Hemsworth's personal trainer, explains the workout he designed for Hemsworth for the film Men in Black: Internationalk (2019). The workout was designed to achieve a less bulky look than Thor and to be doable while travelling, so it involves only bodyweight and a pair of dumbbells. It starts with a warm-up that involves bear crawls (10 rounds of 20 seconds, with resting periods of 10 seconds) and bodyweight squats (also 10 rounds of 20 seconds, with resting periods of 10 seconds). The other exercises are weighted burpee (8 reps), curl to press (8 reps), goblet squats (8 reps), standing triceps extensions (8 reps), reverse lunge curls (8 reps), a shoulder excercise that cycles through a lateral raise, a front raise and an upright row (8 reps), and three plank variations: plank punches (8 reps), plank pulses (8 reps) and plank pikes (8 reps). This set of exercises are done in a cycle for thirty minutes.
    Warning: Skip or replace the upright rows in the shoulder exercise; the upright row demonstrated in this video raises the elbows above shoulder level, which brings your shoulders into internal rotation, and this can be harmful to the shoulder joints.
