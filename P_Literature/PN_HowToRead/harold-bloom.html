Harold Bloom (1930–2019)

(…) our time is limited. We read against the clock. We read, ultimately, in the shadow of mortality. And I think it does matter immensely what you read and how you read it. (Harold Bloom in an interview with Charlies Rose in 2000).

For this new edition, Harold Bloom has added an introductory essay on the historical backgrounds of English Romantic poetry and an epilogue relating his book to literary trends.

No other book on Stevens is as ambitious or comprehensive as this one: everyone who writes on Stevens will have to take it into account. The product of twenty years of meditating, thinking, and writing about Stevens, this truly remarkable book is a brilliant extension of Bloom's theories of literary interpretation.

He feels himself “edged by nothingness,” uncomprehending, but still sustained by reading. Generous and clear-eyed, this is among Harold Bloom’s most ambitious and most moving books.

  • Gray, Rockwell: Harold Bloom'S Short List of Our Culture's Best Books, Chicago Tribune, 29.01.1995. Review of The Western Canon. Gray points out that authors such as Stendahl, Flaubert, Dostoyevsky, James, Mann and Lawrence are not included in Bloom's canon. According to the reviewer, [Bloom] escapes arbitrariness, first by choosing obvious masters and second by arguing that a writer's own "aesthetic strength" determines whether he or she will break into the canon and endure. This strength "is constituted primarily by an amalgam: mastery of figurative language, originality, cognitive power, knowledge, exuberance of diction." He later adds, Though he seems to have read everything, Bloom has in fact cleaved to those texts that nourish his fancy and feed his theory of literary influence. He offers no method or technique; this is not Mortimer Adler on the World's Great Ideas, or I.A. Richards on "How to Read a Page." Instead, Bloom reads for delight, power and solace.
