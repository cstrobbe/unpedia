Was Machiavelli Machiavellian?

Machiavelli's name is ofte used as a by-word for a type of politics in which the end justifies the means. This simplification does not do justice to his legacy. This article will discuss the political and cultural context of Niccolò Machiavelli's life and works, and summarise his political theories.

Cultural and Political Background

Niccolò Machiavelli lived in Florence from 3 May 1469 to 21 June 1527. His political career lasted from 1498 to 1512; most of the works for which he is remembered today were written between 1512 and 1527. Let us first look at some of the major political and cultural events between 1450 and 1227:

May 1453
The Ottomans conquer Constantinople, the capital of the Byzantine Empire.
July 1453
The English are defeated by the French at the Battle of Castillon, the last battle of the Hundred Years' War.
1478
The catholic monarchs Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile establish the Spanish Inquisition.
1486
Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (1463 – 1494) returns to Florence and writes his Oration on the Dignity of Man (first published in 1496), which has sometimes been described as the “Manifesto of the Renaissanc”.
1487
Publication of the witch-hunter's manual Malleus Maleficarum in Speyer, Germany.
January 1492
The fall of Granada concludes the Reconquista and ends Muslim rule on the Iberian peninsula.
October 1492
Christopher Columbus “discovers” America while trying to find a shorter sea route to India.
June 1494
The Treaty of Tordesillas divides the New World between Spain and Portugal (along a meridian 370 leagues west of the Cape Verde islands).
November 1494
The Medici are expelled from Florence; the Florentines establish a “popular” republic with the Dominican friar Girolamo Savonarola as de facto ruler.
1497–1499
The Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama discovers the sea route to India.
May 1498
Girolamo Savonarola and two other friars (Fra Domenico and Fra Silvestro Maruffi) are arrested, hanged and burned on the Piazza della Signoria in Florence.
1498
Leonardo (1452 – 1519) paints The Last Supper the refectory of the Convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan.
1498–1499
Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475 – 1564) creates the Pietà that is now housed in the St. Peter's Basilica in the Vatican.
1502
Piero Soderini (1450 – 1522) is elected gonfaloniere for life.
1504
Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino paints The Marriage of the Virgin, which exemplifies some major principles of High Renaissance art.
1509
Desiderius Erasmus (1466 (?) – 1536) writes Stultitiae Laus or Moriae Encomium / In Praise of Folly (first printed in 1511).
After 1510 (?)
Nicolaus Copernicus (1473 – 1543) writes his De revolutionibus orbium coelestium, printed posthumously in 1543.
September 1512
Piero Soderini is repudiated; Medici rule in Florence is restored.
1514
Albrecht Dürer (1471 – 1528) creates his Melancolia I engraving.
1516
Thomas More 1478–1535) publishes Utopia.
1519 – 1522
first circumnavigation of the world by the Magellan–Elcano expedition.
October 1520
Charles V (1500 – 1558) is crowned as Holy Roman Emperor.
6 May 1527
Sack of Rome by Spanish and German troops led by the Duke of Bourbon. Pope Clement VII is forced to make peace with Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor.
1530
Alessandro de' Medici, “il Moro” is made Duke of Florence by emperor Charles V, thereby bringing an end to the republic of Florence.
1534
Acts of Supremacy establishes Henry VIII as the supreme head of the Church of England.
