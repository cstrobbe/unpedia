Recommendations

  • The best books on Dante recommended by Nick Havely, Five Books, 05.10.2016.
    Havely, author of Dante, recommends the following books:
    • The Divine Comedy I: Inferno translated by Robert M. Durling and Ronlad Martinez (Oxford University Press),
    • Dante: A Brief History by Peter S Hawkins,
    • Dante in English edited by Eric Griffiths & Matthew Reynolds (Penguin, 2005),
    • Dante on View by Antonella Braida and Luisa Calè (2007),
    • Linden Hills, a novel by Gloria Naylor (1985).
    Also mentioned: Allen Mandelbaum's translation of the entire Divine Comedy (published by Everyman's Library), Dante: A Very Short Introduction by Peter Hainsworth and David Robey (2015), Dante's Testaments: Essays in Scriptural Imagination by Peter S. Hawkins (1999), The Dante Club: A Novel by Matthew Pearl (2004) and Dante's British Public: Readers and Texts, from the Fourteenth Century to the Present by Nick Havely (2014)
  • Which is the best English-language translation of Dante Alighieri's "Divina Commedia"?, Quora.
    This question has several answers. Robert Todd says that the translation by Jean and Robert Hollander give us a supple, musical but admirably faithful English that actually works as English verse! Leaner, pithier, more direct than Mendelbaum, yet musical as a prose rendition can never be. John Sinclair’s 1939 prose translation is excellent for those who want an English crib to aid in study of Italian. Robert Durling's translation also stands out for his copious annotations and notes.
    Matt Hodel prefers Mandelbaum's translation, which retains Dante's terza rime. It has less extensive endnotes than some other translations.
    Ani Ravi points out that Mandelbaum's translation is the one used at Columbia for its Core Curriculum, for whatever that's worth. It has minimal endnotes and the Italian on the facing page.
    Michael Masiello prefers John Sinclair’s excellent facing-page translation in prose.
    Matthew Bright writes, John Ciardi’s translation of the Inferno is beyond compare. Mark Musa does a great Purgatorio, and Dorothy Sayers does the best Paradiso, however, my understanding of this question goes back to the 50’s.
    Andrei Estioco also finds John Ciardi's translation the best, whereas Mandelbaum's translation of Purgatorio ended up sounding dry.
    Christopher White points out that the edition with the most exhaustive notes I can think of is translated by Charles S. Singleton (Princeton UP) (six volumes). However, for first-time readers, he recommends John D. Sinclair's translation, whereas the more recent prose edition by Robert M. Durling and Ronald L. Martinez (Oxford UP) could be considered a happy medium between Sinclair and Singleton.
