  27. Ab Exercises Ranked (BEST TO WORST!) (14 minutes, 28.06.2021).
    The ranking is based on the following criteria: how multidimensional they are, how scalable they are (from beginner to advanced), how much equipment they require and how often people do them incorrectly.
    The ranking starts with the worst ab exercises. The Russian twist is not a bad exercise but it is often turned into a Russian tap (without rotation). The bicycle crunch is also meant to be a rotational exercise but is often turned into an exercise that relies on the hip flexors. The dumbbell side bend is an inferior exercise; it is more an obliques exercise than an ab exercise, and it can create problems in the lower back. The plank is not worth doing if you can do it for a minute or longer; moreover, it is often done without activating the glutes, which turns into a hip flexor exercise. The lying leg raise challenges the lower abs, but simply lifting the legs up and down relies too much on the hip flexors and the exercise can cause lower back pain.
    The following exercises are better. The hanging leg raise may fatigue your hands before your abs; it should also be done moving the pelvis up. The ab wheel rollout is also often done incorrectly when moving too far back, thereby taking the tension off the abs.
    The category “better still” has three exercises. The hanging knee raise is better than the hanging leg raise because the hip flexors don't dominate the exercise so much, but it has the same grip strength issue. The hanging cork screw is not a leg lift but a pelvis left and involves the obliques; it also requires good grip strength. The single-sided slow carry also involves the oblliques, preventing a side bend.
    The category “almost best” contains four exercises. The side bridge twist is a better way to do lateral flexion, namely from the bottom up. The levitation crunch is a simple and small movement targetting the upper abs (when done correctly): the goal is to lift the shoulder blades off the ground, without pulling your head towards your knees. Swipers are a good minimalist exercise for the lower abs: the goal is to move the pelvis off the ground so you can swipe your hands below your tailbone. The gymnast ab tuck also focuses on the lower abs.
    The “best” category contains just one exercise: the sliding tuck requires that you curl the pelvis into a posterior pelvic tilt using the abs instead of the hip flexors.

  5. Chest Exercises Ranked (BEST TO WORST!) (15 minutes, 12.07.2021).
    The worst category includes the bench flys (due to the risk of damage to the anterior shoulder capsule and the risk of pec tears), the standing cable press (because it causes more of a challenge to the core than to the chest) and the incline bench press at 60 degrees (or any angle over 50 degrees).
    The better category includes the basic push-up (not challenging enough if you can do more than 30 or 40 reps per set), floor flys (as a safer alternative to bench flys that allows more weight) and the underhand dumbbell bench press.
    The “better still category” includes the twisting push-up (more adduction than the basic push-up), cable cross-overs (more adduction), lying cable bench press (more effective for hypertrophy than strength) and the dumbbell upper chest pull-over (which can be made to work the upper chest when the elbows are squeezed together).
    The “almost best category” includes the dip (the better version of the push-up), heavy one-arm cross-over (fewer balance issues than the cross-overs, so you can use more weight) and the incline bench press as 30–45 degrees.
    The best exercise is the barbell bench press or the dumbbell bench press, which can drive both strength and hypertrophy. The barbell version is better for strength.
    See also ATHLEAN-X: The "Worst" Chest Advice? (Greg Doucette, 14 minutes, 16.07.2021). Greg agrees with some of Jeff's claims (for example, the incline bench press) and disagrees with others. Greg thinks that bench flys aren't as risky as Jeff says, that most people can still benefit from push-ups (you can add bands or weights, or put your feet higher), that floor flys are not a great exercise, that the most people can benefit from cable cross-overs (but he demonstrates a different variation), that the dumbbell pull-over is a lat exercise rather than a chest exercise, that the dip can be hard on the shoulders (especially if you lean forward), that the incline bench press should be done at 15 to 30 degrees, etcetera. Greg also points out that there are several chest exercises using machines that you can do at most gyms, including the pec deck.

  1. How To Build Bigger Biceps with the Athlean X Biceps Blast (9 minutes, 16.07.2009).
    In the introduction, Jeff points out that the AthleanX website has been revamped, after which he explains four biceps exercises that he uses as the foundation of his own workouts. He starts with a straight-bar biceps curl and explains that he allows a little bit of a cheat on this exercise, even though he usually stresses correct form. He demonstrates a biceps curl that focuses on the negative, which takes 4–5 seconds. When the biceps get tired, the (minor) cheat on the upward movements allows more reps focusing on the negative movement.
    He then demonstrates a chin-up in which the upward movement does not go all the way up but only to a 90° angle between upper arm and forearm; at the top of the movement, you should hold for a second, then do a slow negative (doing 8–10 reps).
    The third exercise is like a curl but using tubes instead of a bar; since this is a lighter exercise, the tempo is a bit faster and the rep count is between 30 and 40. The advantage of a resistance band compared to a bar is the different strength curve: with a bar, the hardest part is when the forearm is parallel to the floor, whereas with a band, the movement becomes harder as the forearms move to the top of the movement.
    The final exercise is a dead-arm curl: since the easiest position in a regular curl is at the top, this curl exercise is done standing in a baseball fielder position, i.e. bent over, so that the hardest part is at the top of the movement. In this exercise, too, you should focus on the negative.

You can knock out 9-12 sets of each in less than 20 minutes!

    Biceps are a small muscle group and don't need much volume to stimulate growth! Perform 1 round of each of the exercises (minimizing the rest in between to just enough that allows you to recover for the next exercise)...then rest 2 minutes in between the rounds. Recovery and Avoiding Losing Gains

  1. Don’t Ignore THIS Early Warning Sign - MUSCLE LOSS!! (7 minutes, 17.07.2019)
    Grip strength is neuromuscular driven. A loss of grip strength can be a sign that you are overtraining or under-recovering. You can test grip strength using a special device, a hand grip dynamometer, which is rather expensive. Instead, you can also use a bodyweight scale to get a good approximation of what the dynamometer would say. If you notice a signification drop in grip strength, i.e. a 10% drop, you need to change something in your training, such as reducing the volume or taking a deload week.

Other Workout Tips and Advice

  1. 1 Tip To More Muscle - Find Out How Long Your Workout Should Be (4 minutes, 16.11.2009).
    If you want to build muscle, you need a “time under tension” of about 45–60 seconds. If each rep lasted four seconds and you did 12 reps, your muscles would be under tension for 48 seconds; that is the origin of the magic number 12. Tension exists both in the positive and the negative portion of a repetition; for example, when doing pullups, you could do the reps 2 seconds up and 2 seconds down, or 1 second up and 3 seconds down. However, a negative movement of three seconds is more challenging. If you spend only 20 seconds on a set, as many people do, you miss opportunities for building muscle.

  8. Muscle Soreness Explained (IS IT GOOD?) (6 minutes, 27.01.2017).
    Muscle soreness is generally created by eccentric training (which Jeff incorrectly pronounces as “ecentric”). Concentric movement shortens the muscle; eccentric movement, which elongates the muscles, causes microtears in the muscle fibres and sparks muscle growth. Concentric training can be done without causing much soreness (or any at all), but the negative movement (for example in a curl) is an eccentric movement, so it needs to be slowed down instead of letting the weight go. Certain exercises, such as cleans and snatches, don't have much of an eccentric movement and can therefore often be done without causing soreness.
    According to Jeff, soreness is necessary for muscle growth. But if you are training for strength, soreness is not necessarily what you're after. You don't need soreness to get stronger, but if you are training for hypertrophy, you may want muscle soreness. However, Jeff warns that if you train 30 to 60 minutes per day, soreness will only lead to muscle growth if you get everythinng else right outside your training, such as eating and getting enough sleep (for recovery). These are areas where people are often less committed.
  9. Muscle Soreness and Muscle Growth (“BROSCIENCE” REVEALED!) (10 minutes, 19.07.2017).
    Some people claim that muscle soreness has nothing to do with muscle growth; this is broscience. Jeff explains three ways in which the body creates muscle growth. Muscle soreness may be necessary eventually when the two other pathways become ineffective.
    The fist pathway is progressive overload, using either tension (adding weight) or volume as the driver. Weight cannot be added indefinitely, so people resort to increased volume. But this is not necessarily a good thing, because it can cause overuse injuries. Increading volume requires spotless form.
    The second pathway is metabolic stress, which is characterised by byproducts such as lactate and hydrogen ions. This method allows lower loads but requires fortitude to train through the pain and the burn. Occlusion training also relies on this. This method requires either continuous tension or contracted position exercises (such as the spider curl).
    The third pathway relies on muscle mechanical damage caused by eccentric overload. This method is easy to apply: you simply slow down the eccentric movement. The downside of the soreness caused by this is that you may not be able to train as frequently as otherwise.
    Muscle soreness is not a prerequsite for muscle growth but will eventually be part of the equation. You will need each of the three methods.
  10. 8 Worst Bodyweight Exercises Ever (STOP DOING THESE!) (12 minutes, 08.03.2020).

The third exercise is the pull-up but at a slower speed and while tensing as many other muscles as possible. After this exercise, you squeeze and hold the back for six seconds (two or three times). The fourth exercise is a constant-tension chin-up with a slow negative movement. This is followed by a six-second biceps squeeze. The fifth exercise is a constant-tension version of the cobra push-up, followed up by a six-second triceps contraction. The sixth exercise is a handstand push-up, followed by an isometric contraction of the delts. The final exercise is constant-tension eccentric step-up and step-down at a slow cadence.
