    • Top 10 Scientific Training Tips to Maximize Muscle Growth and to Bring up Weak Body-parts EXPLAINED! (17 minutes, 11.05.2019).
      1. Always warm up with light to moderate cardio. You should be slightly sweating at the start of the training session.
      2. Train the hardest exercises first, instead of pre-fatiguing muscles. For example, if squats are the hardest exercise in the workout, do these first.
      3. Instead of counting reps, time your sets, since “time under tension” is what counts. For muscle hypertrophy, the time under tension should be around 40 seconds; a shorter time under tension benefits strength rather than hypertrophy. But sets should not be shorter than 20 seconds.
      4. The work-to-rest ratio should be between three-to-one and five-to-one. For example, if a set takes one minute (which is long), the rest period should be between three and five minutes. Shorter periods turn the workout into toning or cardio training and force you to use lower weights.
      5. Make the eccentric phase longer than the concentric phase; ideally twice as long. If you do 12 reps that last three seconds each, you get close to 40 seconds of time under tension.
      6. Train hard sets close to failure. (If you can do five sets of 10 reps, the first four sets were too light.)
      7. Use proper form. Otherwise you either don't hit the right muscles, or you get injured.
      8. Active rest helps clearing lactic acid from the muscles.
      9. Train each body part twice a week, especially weak body parts. A small muscle such as the biceps can handle between 15 and 20 sets per week, which means 7 to 10 sets per session. Bigger muscles such as the back and the legs can handle 20 to 30 sets per week. The exact number depends on the person, training experiecen and other factors.
      10. Always stretch after a workout in order to prevent tightness and injuries. (Stretching before a workout makes you weaker.)

Training Advice

For Beginners

  • Suggestions For Lifting Beginners (Every Damn Day Fitness on YouTube, 6 minutes, 09.05.2018).
    Alan Roberts sells several training programmes on his site, for example E.D.D.F. LEAN MASS HYPERTROPHY PROGRAM and E.D.D.F. VOLUME TRAINING. In this no-nonsense video, he explains that he once considered creating a programme for beginners and why he decided not to do that after all. Beginners should learn the four basic lifts (the bench press, the squat, the deadlift and the overhead press), but these exercises shouldn't be taught using distance learning. The reason is that there are both mental and physical variables that need to be taken into account by a coach who is physically present can correct (and a video can't). Physiological variablese that need to be taken into account include height, weight, age, body fat percentage, the length of the femur, even the length of the femur's neck.
    For this reason, Alan Roberts recommends that beginners should find a strength coach who can teach the four basic lifts in person. After about six months of getting proficient at these exercises, beginners can start worrying about curls or a novice programme, i.e. a three-times-per-week full-body programme that builds on the four basic lifts. Males who have between 12 and 20% body fat shouldn't worry too much about nutrition as long as they stay withing that range. If beginners start aiming for caloric deficits in order to make their abs visible, they will rob themselves of what Alan Roberts calls "noob gains" or lean mass gains. As long as you eat healthy (with sufficient protein, i.e. 1 gram of protein per pound of lean body mass) and work out hard using the four basic lifts, you will develop a good-looking physique. Alan Robets doesn't believe in supplemenation for beginners; they should be getting enough protein through regular food. He stresses the fact that training needs to be consistent: fitness is a lifestyle.

High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)

  • High-intensity interval training (HIIT), Wikipedia (accessed on 17.06.2021).
  • Does HIIT STOP Effective Muscle Growth? (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 9 minutes, 31.05.2021).
    Some people claim that you shouldn't do HIIT if you are in a muscle-building cycle. In other words, if you have concurrent protocols, one may inhibit the other. James refers to the paper The effects of high intensity interval training on muscle size and quality in overweight and obese adults by Malia N. M. Blue, Abbie E. Smith-Ryan, Eric T. Trexler and Katie H. Hirsch (Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport, June 2017), but this is about a very specific population. He therefore looks at two other papers: Effects of Concurrent Power Training and High-Intensity Interval Cycling on Muscle Morphology and Performance by Polyxeni Spiliopoulou et al (Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, April 2019; abstract only) and Effects of high-intensity interval cycling performed after resistance training on muscle strength and hypertrophy by Tsitkanou et al (Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports, 2017).
    The paper by Tsitkanou et al is about an eight-week study which found that the HIIT did not negatively impact on the muscle growth and strength growth potential. However, the group that did HIIT experienced a decrease in rate of force development (RFD), which can be disadvantage to athletes. But the advantage of the HIIT was increase in capillary density, which can be beneficiary for the aerobic system. The study does not support the claim that HIIT inhibits muscle growth.
    The paper by Spiliopoulou et al is about a six-week study in which a power training protocol was followed by HIIT (three times per week). The group who did HIIT suffered inhibition to their countermovement jump power performance; this is consistent with the decrease in RFD found in the study by Tsitkanou et al.
    James says there is room for further research in this area. He also looks at the meta-analysis Concurrent training: a meta-analysis examining interference of aerobic and resistance exercises by Jacob M. Wilson et al (Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 2012), but this looked at endurance training, not HIIT per se. This meta-analysis found that athletes whose sport requires maximal power or rate of force development should limit concurrently training for strength and endurance. However, if an athlete's sport is primarily dependent on maximal strength and hypertrophy, then concurrent training may not lead to significant decrements, given the proper modality of endurance training is selected.

  • How Long Should You Diet To Get A Six-Pack? (AVOID THIS MISTAKE!) (Jeremy Ethier on YouTube, 13 minutes, 04.04.2021).
    The amount of body fat you need to get rid off to make a six-pack visible varies based on several factors. The video presents a formula to calculate the “time to six-pack”: bodyweight goal = lean body weight / (1 - desired body fat percentage). To determine your lean bodyweight, you need to estimate your bodyfat percentage (roughly visually) and divide this by your current bodyweight, then substract that number from your bodyweight. Then pick your goal bodyfat percentage; you can be conservative here, since you can always push on later. (The video chooses 12% as an example.) Based on this, you get the bodyweight you would have with the desired bodyfat percentage. Based on this, you can determine how much fat you need to lose.
    Don't make the mistake of trying to lose that weight as fast as possible. There is a limit to the amount of weight you can lose per day; beyond that limit, you will lose weight by losing muscle tissue. Jeremy refers to the study Effect of two different weight-loss rates on body composition and strength and power-related performance in elite athletes by Ina Garthe and others (2011). Based on this, Jeremy recommends losing 0.7% of your current bodyweight per week.
    You need to be consistent and expect ups and downs. Many people use diet breaks to help reach their weight-loss goal.

However, most carnivores find meat tastier when it contains a bit more fat, i.e. around 20%. In that scenario, you would get around 26% of your calories from protein and 74% from fat. When increasing the fat percentage in the meat you eat to 25%, you get around 23% of your calories from protein and around 77% from fat. None of these numbers will give you protein toxicity. However, if you ate extremely lean protein, say meat containing 98% protein and 2% fat, you'd get 81% of your calories from protein and only around 19% from fat. This would result in “rabbit starvation”. Turning a lot of protein into glucose is very unnatural process for the human body; it is also rather inefficient, so you need to eat more. There is also a limit to the amount of protein the liver can convert into glucose; when the body reaches that limit, the remaining protein cannot be converted.
