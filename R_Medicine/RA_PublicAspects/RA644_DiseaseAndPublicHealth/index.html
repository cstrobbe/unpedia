  • Crise des masques : comment la France a arrêté de se préparer aux épidémies, (Le Monde on YouTube, 10 minutes, 21.04.2020).
    France created a stock of surgical masks and FFP2 masks after the 2005 H5N1 (“bird flu”) outbreak but after the 2009 swine flu epidemic did not have much of an impact on the country, the stockpile of surgical masks was seriously reduced and the stockpile of FFP2 masks was eliminated. As a consequence, France was insufficiently prepared at the start of the Covid-19 pandemic.

Other Resources

  • Huet, Natalie; Paun, Carmen: Meet the world’s most powerful doctor: Bill Gates, Politico, 04.05.2017.
    Bill Gates has become the second biggest donor to the WHO (behind the USA), which gives him a lot of influence over the organisation's agenda. According to critics, the WHO's priorities have shifted: the agency spends a disproportionate amount of its resources on projects with the measurable outcomes Gates prefers, such as the effort to eradicate polio. Because of Gates' influence, few people dare criticise him openly. Some health advocates fear that because the Gates Foundation’s money comes from investments in big business, it could serve as a Trojan horse for corporate interests to undermine WHO’s role in setting standards and shaping health policies.
    The Gates Foundation has pumped more than $2.4 billion into the WHO since 2000, as countries have grown reluctant to put more of their own money into the agency, especially after the 2008 global financial crisis. The Gates Foundation's emphasis on polio instead of resilient health systems has effectively left WHO begging for funding for other programs, particularly to prop up poor countries’ health systems before the next epidemic hits.

  • The Economics of Coronavirus: A Reading List recommended by Ricardo Reis, Five Books, 29.04.2020.
    Ricardo Reis, professor of economics at the London School of Economics, says that the Covid-19 pandemic is a unique phenomenon but that it is still useful to look at past experiences such as the 1918 Spanish flu pandemic and World War II. However, from an economic point of view there are a number of known unknowns (for example about publlic debt, safety nets, financial markets and people's expectations) and unkown unknowns. He recommends books as well as an article because economic theory tends to be published in journal articles, whereas economic historians publish more books:
    • Free Trade Under Fire by Douglas A. Irwin is essentially a defence of free trade.
    • A Monetary History of the United States, 1867-1960 by Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz. This book shows that the USA's Central Bank did very different things from today. Reis also recommends the article The Treasury Fed Accord: A New Narrative Account by Bob Hetzel and Rich Leach (2001).
    • “The Liquidation of Government Debt” by Carmen Reinhart and Belen Sbrancia (Economic Policy, Volume 30, Issue 82, April 2015). (Carmen Reinhart is co-author of This Time Is Different about sovereign debt crises.)
    • The Great Reversal: How America Gave Up on Free Markets by Thomas Philippon.
    • Hard Head, Soft Hearts: Tough-minded Economics for a Just Society by Alan S. Blinder.
