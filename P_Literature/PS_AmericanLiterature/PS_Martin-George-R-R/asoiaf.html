A Song of Ice and Fire / Game of Thrones

Historical Sources and Inspiration

  • History Behind Game of Thrones (website maintained by Jamie Adair).
  • Mayes T.: The Real History Behind Game Of Thrones - Part 1 (YouTube, 18 minutes) and The Real History Behind Game Of Thrones - Part 2 (YouTube, 22 minute).
    Two important sources are the Wars of the Roses in 15th-century England and Maurice Druon's series of historival novels The Accursed Kings (Les Rois maudits). Valyrian steel was inspired by Damascus steel, whose method of production has been lost. The Red Wedding was inspired by the Black Dinner (1440) and the Massacre of Glencoe (1692). Cersei's walk of shame was inspired by Jane Shore's penance walk in the late 15th century. The Wall was inspired by Hadrian's Wall. The Knight's Watch was inspired by military orders such as the Knights Hospitaller and the Knights Templar. The Dothraki were inspired by nomadic people like the Huns and the Mongols. The Unsullied were inspired by slave armies such as the Mamluks and the Janissaries. The fighting pits of Meereen were inspired by the gladiator games in Ancient Rome. The Sons of the Harpy use daggers and know how to blend into the crowd after attacking people; this is reminiscent of the Sicarii in Jerusalem in the first century AD.
  • History Buffs: Why Game of Thrones reads like a History Book and not a Fantasy Epic (YouTube, 20 minutes).
  • Kyle Kallgren: Game of Thrones and History - Between the Lines (YouTube, 10 minutes, July 2014).
    Kyle Kallgren cites Umberto Eco's paper Dreaming of the Middle Ages (1987). See also neo-medievalism on Wikipedia.
  • TopTenzNet: Top 10 Ways Game of Thrones Ripped Off Chinese History (YouTube, 17 minutes, 07.06.2015). This video contains several factual errors. For example, it claims that the Great Wall of China is 20,000 kilometres long and that it was constructed in several phases, all Chinese names are mispronounced, and it locates the kingdom of Qin in the West of China. However, it also draws parallels between Robert Baratheon and Emperor Wenxuan of Northern Qi, between Cersei Lannister and Empress Dowager Cixi, between Aegon IV Targaryen and the Ming dynasty Zhengde Emperor, between Joffrey Baratheon and Liu Ziye, who is also known as Emperor Qianfei of the Liu Song dynasty, between The Rains of Castamere and the 1645 Yangzhou massacre (there was another Yangzhou massacre in 760), and between the struggle between Cersei Lannyster and Margaery Tyrell on the one hand and Empress Lü Zhi and Consort Qi on the other.

There may be more historical inspirations than are mentioned in the above sources. For example, the family name Martell is almost identical to Charles Martel's cognomen (“hammer”; “Martell” in German). Charles Martel fought important battles against Islamic forces, which has a kind of parallel to Dorne's resistance against the Targaryens, who also come from the east. Was Robert Baratheon's war hammer inspired by Charles Martel?

Was the battle of Qohor, in which Dothraki cavalry charges again and again on the Unsullied inspired by the battle of Tours, in which the Islamic cavalry repeatedly charged against the Frankish infantry? (See this YouTube video about the Battle of Tours/Poitiers in 732.)

The family name Lannister sounds similar to Lancaster, one of the houses in the Wars of the Roses in 15th-century England. Tywin Lannister's first name looks almost identical to the name of the Welsh town Tywyn, but the pronunciation is quite different.

King Edward IV of England (1442 – 1483) was a good soldier, but once he got hold of the throne, he became more interested in women (he had numerous mistresses and illegitimate children) and alchohol. In this respect, he is similar to Robert Baratheon.

Women who fight (Brienne of Tarth) or lead an army (Daenerys Targaryen) also figure in Shakespeare's Henry VI: in Henry VI, Part 1 Joan of Arc helps lift the Siege of Orléans, and in Henry VI, Part 3 Queen Margaret, Henry VI's wife, was present at some of the battles in the Wars of the Roses.

The direwolves are based on the real dire wolf (Canis dirus), which became extinct 9,000 years ago. The dire wolf was roughly the size of the largerst modern gray wolf, but with larger teeth and a greater bite force.

Literary Sources and Inspiration

  • PBS NewsHour: What does a medieval lit scholar see in 'Game of Thrones?' (YouTube, 7 minutes): Brantley L. Bryant, associate professor of early and medieval British literature at Sonoma State University, discusses elements of medieval literature in the HBO TV show.
  • The pie made out of his two sons that Walder Frey eats was probably inspired by the end of Shakespeare's play Titus Andronicus, where Titus serves Tamora a pie made from the flesh of her sons Chiron and Demetrius.

When Will A Song of Ice and Fire Be Finished?

When the TV series Game of Thrones started running on HBO in April 2011, George R. R. Martin had published 4 novels in the series A Song of Ice and Fire:

  • A Game of Thrones (1996, 94 pages)
  • A Clash of Kings (1998, 768 pages)
  • A Storm of Swords (2000, 973 pages)
  • A Feast for Crows (2005, 976 pages)

A Dance with Dragons followed in July 2011. George R. R. Martin announced the publication of the sixth novel, The Winds of Winter, several times. It would be published at any time between 2014 and 2018, but the publication has been postponed several times. This has given rise to speculation about what is delaying the completion of the novel and about whether George R. R. Martin would finish the series at all.

  • TheOhioHeroinEpidemic1: Our interview with George R.R. Martin in San Jose (YouTube, 6 minutes, 23.07.2017). This interview dates from a point in time when the author had a plan for six volumes and finished two of them.
  • Beaumont-Thomas, Ben: George RR Martin's editor hints at eighth Game of Thrones book, The Guardian, 03.06.2014.
  • The Last Harpy: Winds Of Winter Release UPDATE / GRRM Frustrated by Fans! (Game of Thrones) (YouTube, 31.07.2016, 5 minutes).
  • Mateao12485: The REAL Reason Why "The Winds of Winter" Is Taking So Long, and Why Game of Thrones Cut Characters (YouTube, 02.10.2016, 5 minutes). Points out that George R. R. Martin has constantly been story building in the first four books and that he loses interest in a book once he knows how it will end. In the end, there may be more than seven books, since story building continues in the fifth novel.
