A Companion to “Shakespeare and His World”

Introduction

This collection of web resources serves as a “companion” to the MOOC Shakespeare and His World. The course was created by Professor Jonathan Bate (Warwick University) in co-operation with the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust. Jonathan Bate is the MOOC's lead educator, and he is sometimes joined by Jennifer Reid of the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust.

The course ran for the first time in March–May 2014 and had around 11,000 registered learners (see Join us in celebrating 450 years of Shakespeare, Warwick Business School, 02 September 2014). The MOOC had its fifth run on FutureLearn from 20 March till 27 May 2017; some of the participants are taking the course for the fifth time, for the quality of the course content itself, the lively and interesting comments or both. At the time of writing, it is not clear whether the course will be repeated.

The MOOC is a 10-week course, with each week focusing on a specific theme. Each week is subdivided in “steps”: one or two introductory steps; six steps with a video that presents an object, usually from the collections of the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust; an assessment that consists of 12 multiple-choice questions; a discussion step where learners can comment on their favourite object of the week; and a summary step with a video in which Jonathan Bate and Jennifer Reid discuss certain themes and ideas from the week. Except for the assessments, each step has a comments section. The number of comments in each step varies between just a few dozen and several hundreds. Many of these comments contain links to other interesting resources. They also contain enough book recommendations to break anyone's book-buying budget.
You can see the first step of the first week (without the comments, which are not public) on the FutureLearn website.

Weeks 2 to 9 have a focus play, and the objects presented in the videos are used to illustrate certain excerpts and themes from the plays. It is a good idea, though not a requirement, to read the plays before the start of the week. For those learners who don't have the time to read the entire plays, Jonathan Bate provides a list of four or five scenes that are important to read in the context of the course.

Shakespeare's plays can be read online, for example on the following websites:

  • Open Source Shakespeare, a website that does not only contain most of Shakespeare's works but also advanced search functions and a concordance. This is the electronic edition recommended by Jonathan Bate.
  • Plays and Poems on the website “Internet Shakespeare Editions” hosted at the University of Victoria in Canada and possibly the oldest Shakespeare website that is still online.
  • Shakespeare's Words: Works: the texts on this website are those from the editions published by Penguin Books (except for Cymbeline and Edward III). These texts have annotations in the margins, which is helpful for reading the plays online.
  • The Complete Works of William Shakespeare, a website hosting the digital Complete Moby(tm) Shakespeare. (See How Moby Shakespeare Took Over the Internet for more background about this edition.)
  • The Oxford Shakespeare, a digitised version of the edition prepared by W. J. Craig and originally published in 1914 (and therefore outdated).

However, since Shakespeare's works contain many words, expressions and allusions that are obscure to most modern readers, it is a good idea to read a thoroughly annotated edition of each play. There is a wide choice of annotated editions, for example (in no particular order):

  • The Arden Shakespeare, currently in the hands of Bloomsbury Group;
  • The New Cambridge Shakespeare, published by Cambridge University Press;
  • The Oxford Shakespeare, published by Oxford University Press;
  • The New Penguin Shakespeare, published by Penguin;
  • The RSC Shakespeare, published by Palgrave Macmillan;
  • The Folger Shakespeare Library Editions, published by the Folger Shakespeare Library;
  • the Shakespeare plays in the series Norton Critical Editions, published by W. W. Norton. Not all Shakespeare plays are available in this series. It has editions of the following focus plays: The Merchant of Venice, Othello, Macbeth, Antony and Cleopatra, The Tempest, but not The Merry Wives of Windsor, A Midsummer Night's Dream and Henry V.
  • The Shakespeare plays published as Collins Classics. (The text corresponds to the edition by Peter Alexander, originally published by Collins in 1951: William Shakespeare: The Complete Works.)

Which edition you choose depends on how seriously you want to study Shakespeare, how much convenience you want and how much money you want to spend on books. The New Penguin Shakespeare is relatively cheap, but the notes are at the end of the book instead of the bottom of the page. The Arden, Cambridge, Oxford, RSC, Folger and Norton editions have notes at the bottom of the page. These editions generally also have more extensive introductions, including explanations about the origin of the text and the editorial procedures. So the convenience of having notes on the same page as the text comes brings additional materials that would mainly interest serious students and scholars. The Norton Critical Editions contain an annotated text of the play, a section with comments by actors, a section with extracts from sources used by Shakespeare, a selection of criticism, and sometimes also excerpts from later works that were inspired by the play.

The Shakespeare editions in the Collins Classics series sometimes have a glossary at the end of the volume (e.g. Othello, The Merchant of Venice, Hamlet, King Lear, Julius Caesar, As You Like It and The Taming of the Shrew, ), while other plays have notes pages facing the text (e.g. Henry V, A Midsummer Night's Dream, Macbeth, Much Ado About Nothing, Twelfth Night and Romeo and Juliet).

It can also be helpful to consult a glossary with word definitions that have changed since Shakespeare's time. Several glossaries are available, both online and in print:

  • Crystal, David; Crystal, Ben: Shakespeare's Words: A Glossary and Language Companion. Penguin, 2002. (650 pages) ISBN 0 14 100737 0.
    The book also has a website: Shakespeare's Words.
  • Onions, C. T.; Eagleson, Robert D. (revised edition): A Shakespeare Glossary. Third edition. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1986. ISBN 9780198125211.
  • The Shakespeare Glossary on the website Shakespeare Online.

Open Source Shakespeare also has a Concordance of Shakespeare's complete works.

The Shakespeare Birthplace Trust

The course uses resources from the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust, so here are a few relevant links:

  • Shakespeare Birthplace Trust: the trust's home page. The Shakespeare Birthplace Trust is a registered charity based in Stratford-upon-Avon, Shakespeare's home town. They now work according to the terms set out in the Shakespeare Birthplace Act of 1961 (PDF<).
  • Finding Shakespeare: a website set up by the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust to provide online access to their collections. It has several sections or “archives”, for example Shakespeare and his World, Shakespeare's Life and Shakespeare in 100 Objects.

Performing Shakespeare Indoors

Gurr, Andrew; Karim-Cooper, Farah (editors): Moving Shakespeare Indoors: Performance and Repertoire in the Jacobean Playhouse. Cambridge University Press, 2014. (308 pages) 9781107040632.
The year 2014, in which this book was published, saw the opening of the Sam Wanamaker Playhouse, which is based on seventeenth-century designs of an indoor London theatre, of which Blackfriars was another example.

In the first scene of The Merchant of Venice, Antonio explains that he is said and that he does not know why. His friends try to find an explanation, but he finally says:

I hold the world but as the world, Gratiano;
A stage where every man must play a part,
And mine a sad one.

The Merchant of Venice, Act I, scene 1, lines 82–84.

Some scholars have tried to explain Antonio's melancholy temperament as a consequence of homosexuality (towards Bassanio, who will leave Venice to woo Portia), which could not be expressed in the play. It can also be seen as serving a theatrical purpose, namely to prepare the audience for Antonio's apathetic attitude in later scenes, especially during the trial scene without the need to make the character evolve psychologically during the play.

Is Antonio melancholy in the sense of the theory of the four temperament or humours that Shakespeare often alludes to in his plays? Shakespeare does not make this explicit. For more about the four humours in Elizabethan literature, see

  • Morris, Sylvia: Sadness and the four humours in Shakespeare, The Shakespeare Blog, 18 February 2014.
  • Paster, Gail Kern: William Shakespeare and the Four Humours: Elizabethan Medical Beliefs, YouTube video of a 55-minute lecture given at the University of Pittsburgh on 2 October 2014.
  • Paster, Gail Kern; Rowe, Katherine; Floyd-Wilson, Mary (editors): Reading the Early Modern Passions: Essays in the Cultural History of Emotion. University of Pennsylvania Press, 2004. (392 pages) ISBN 9780812218725.
  • Paster, Gail Kern: Humoring the Body: Emotions and the Shakespearean Stage. University of Chicago Press, 2010 (2014). (290 pages) ISBN 9780226213828.

The Merchant of Venice is a play with a lot of legal language, not only in the scenes that involve Antonio and Shylock (e.g. the trial scene). However, it is not the only play where law plays a role; other plays include Othello, King Lear, The Winter's Tale and Richard II.

  • Zurcher, Andrew (editor): Shakespeare and Law. The Arden Shakespeare. Bloomsbury, 2010. (352 pages) ISBN 9781904271727.
  • Cormack, Bradin; Nussbaum, Martha C.; Strier, Richard (editors): Shakespeare and the Law: A Conversation among Disciplines and Professions. University of Chicago Press, 2013 (2016). (344 pages) ISBN 9780226378565.
  • NYU Libraries: Shakespeare Studies: Shakespeare and Law: a selection of titles in the NYU libraries that are relevant to law and politics in Shakespeare's plays. This page is part of the larger Shakespeare Studies website.
