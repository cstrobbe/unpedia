The Epic of Gilgamesh

Editions and Translations

The authoritative edition of the Standard Babylonian version of the epic is the following:
The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic: Introduction, Critical Edition and Cuneiform Texts. Edited by Andrew R. George. (Two volumes) Oxford University Press, 2003. ISBN 9780198149224.
According to the publisher, Since the last critical edition of the epic seventy years ago the known fragments of the epic have almost doubled. This book collects all the extant texts in one place again, including twenty-three fragments published for the first time. The author has studied personally every available fragment to produce a definitive edition and translation. Four introductory chapters place the epic in its context and examine the name, person and traditions of Gilgamesh and other characters in the poem. The plates present the cuneiform text of all the extant fragments of the epic.

Newer translations are based on Andrew George's edition, sometimes with the addition of clay tablet fragments discovered since 2003. All translations published before 2003 are now outdated.
The translations below are listed by order of publication.

Parpola, Simo: The Standard Babylonian Epic of Gilgamesh. State Archives of Assyria Cuneiform Texts. Eisenbrauns, 1997. (200 pages) ISBN 978-951-45-7760-4.
According to the publisher's description, This is the first in a series of volumes presenting in typeset cuneiform facsimile texts from the Library of Assurbanipal. In addition to an introduction that discusses the sources for the text, Parpola presents the text, a composite transliteration, glossary, indexes, and a sign list. A variant apparatus details significant variants in the different sources for the text.

Note that translations of Akkadian texts that are more than thirty years old are typically out of date. The general reason for this is that the understanding of the language improves as more manuscripts are discovered and read. In addition, Assyriologist regularly discover clay tablet fragments containing previously unknown passages of the Epic of Gilgamesh.

  • The Epic of Gilgamesh. Translated by William Muss-Arnolt. This translation was originally published in 1901.
  • Modernised version of the translation of William Muss-Arnolt: this updated and revised translation [incorporates] additional material found on two Babylonian tablets known as the Pennsylvania and Yale tablets, translated in 1920 by Morris Jastrow, Jr. and Albert T. Clay, as well as other fragments made by L. W. King in Babylonian Religion and Mythology (1903).
  • Reginald Campbell Thompson Translation, originally published in 1930 and now available on the Internet Archive.
  • The Epic of Gilgamesh. An English version with an introduction by N. K. Sandars. Revised edition incorporating new material. London: Penguin Books, 1972. ISBN 9780140441000.
    This is a prose version that focuses on providing a continuous narrative.
  • Gilgamesh. Edited by John Gardner. Translated by John Maier. Vintage, 1985. (320 pages) ISBN 9780394740898.
  • L'Épopée de Gilgameš. Le grand homme qui ne voulait pas mourir. Traduit de l'akkadien par Jean Bottéro. Introduction de Jean Bottéro. Collection L'aube des peuples. Gallimard, 1992. (304 pages) ISBN 9782070725830.
    The French assyriologist Jean Bottéro was one of the greatest experts on the Ancient Near East.
  • The Epic of Gilgamesh: The Babylonian Epic Poem and Other Texts in Akkadian and Sumerian. Translated with an introduction by Andrew George. London: Penguin, 1999. (lv + 228 pages) ISBN 9780140449198.
    Andrew George, who also edited the cuneiform texts, provides a translation with editorial annotations that clearly indicates where words or even entire lines are missing and where text from other tablets (e.g. the Yale tablet and the Pennsylvania tablet) have been used to fill in missing gaps. The assyriologist did not add a translation of the twelfth tablet of the Standard Babylonian version immediately after the eleven other tablets; instead, he added translations of the five older Sumerian Gilgamesh texts at the end of the book. Benjamin Foster said in an interview in 2017, The English translation of Andrew George is by far the most authoritative in any language.
  • Hines, Derrek: Gilgamesh. Chatto and Windus, 2002. (80 pages) ISBN 9780701172527.
    This is a retelling rather than a translation by the Canadian poet Derrek Hines.
  • Das Gilgamesch-Epos. Neu Übersetzt und kommentiert von Stefan M. Maul. München: C. H. Beck, 2005. Sixth edition, 2014. ISBN 978-3-406-52870-5.
    According to the publisher's description, Stefan M. Maul bietet auf der Grundlage von zum Teil noch unpublizierten Textzeugen eine vollständig neue Übersetzung des Gilgamesch-Epos. The translation includes an introduction and annotations. Stefan M. Maul is professor in assyriology at the University of Heidelberg.
  • Das Gilgamesch-Epos. Übersetzt, kommentiert und herausgegeben von Wolfgang Röllig. Reclam Bibliothek. Stuttgart: Philipp Reclam jun. Stuttgart, 2009. ISBN 978-3-15-010702-7.
    In addition to the Standard Babylonian version (without the twelfth tablet), the book also includes translations of a few other fragments.
  • The Epic of Gilgamesh. Translated and edited by Benjamin R. Foster. Second Edition. Norton, 2019. (256 pages) ISBN 978-0-393-64398-5.
    This revised edition includes a translation (without the twelfth tablet) of over 150 recently discovered lines of the epic and retranslations of the Sumerian Gilgamesh poems that take into account new information about the material. The new lines include the so-called monkey tablet. In the translations of the Sumerian Gilgamesh poems, Gilgamesh and Huwawa A and Gilgamesh and Huwawa B were merged into a single text. The book includes the Hittite Gilgamesh and three excerpts from papers or book chapters by scholars. The book does not include a map of ancient Mesopotamia.
  • Gilgamesh. A New Verse Rendering by Stanley Lombardo. Introduction by Gary Beckman. Hackett, 2019. (128 pages) ISBN 978-1-62466-772-5.
    Publisher's description: This stirring new version of the great Babylonian epic includes material from the recently discovered “monkey tablet” as well as an Introduction, timeline, glossary, and correspondences between lines of the translation and those of the original texts. Note that Stanley Lombardo is a classicist, not an assyriologist.
  • Beckman, Gary M.: The Hittite Gilgamesh. Lockwood Press, 2019. (112 pages) ISBN 9781948488068.
    This book offers a full edition of the manuscripts from Hattusa in the Hittite, Akkadian, and Hurrian languages recounting Gilgamesh's adventures; in other words, it is a scholarly addition of the manuscripts, not simply a translation.

Books about the Epic of Gilgamesh

  • Damrosch, David: The Buried Book: The Loss and Rediscovery of the Great Epic of Gilgamesh. Holt, 2007. (336 pages) ISBN 9780805087253.
    From the publisher's description: David Damrosch begins with the rediscovery of the epic in 1872 and from there goes backward in time, all the way to Gilgamesh himself.
  • Ziolkowski, Theodore: Gilgamesh among Us: Modern Encounters with the Ancient Epic. Cornell University Press, 2011. (248 pages) ISBN 9780801450358.
    From the publisher's book description: Ziolkowski sees fascination with Gilgamesh as a reflection of eternal spiritual values—love, friendship, courage, and the fear and acceptance of death. Noted writers, musicians, and artists from Sweden to Spain, from the United States to Australia, have adapted the story in ways that meet the social and artistic trends of the times.
    See also Louis A. Ruprecht, Jr's review of Gilgamesh among Us (Journal of the American Academy of Religion, Volume 81, Issue 3, September 2013.
  • Schmidt, Michael: Gilgamesh: The Life of a Poem. Princeton University Press, 2019. (192 pages) ISBN 9780691195247.
    For reviews, see John Butler: “Gilgamesh: The Life of a Poem” by Michael Schmidt, Asian Review of Books, 26.09.2019, Joan Acocella: How to Read “Gilgamesh”, The New Yorker, 07.10.2019, and David Gullette: Book Review: “Gilgamesh: The Life of a Poem” — A Dazzling Study of the Oldest Long Poem in the World, The Arts Fuse, 16.10.2019.
  • Cregan-Reid, Vybarr: Discovering Gilgamesh: Geology, narrative and the historical sublime in Victorian culture. Manchester University Press, 2013. (256 pages) ISBN 978-0-7190-9051-6.
    This book is about George Smith, who discovered the “deluge tablet” that is part of the epic of Gilgamesh.
  • Sallaberger, Walther: Das Gilgamesch-Epos. Mythos, Werk und Tradition. 2., durchgesehene und aktualisierte Auflage. C. H. Beck Wissen. München: C. H. Beck, 2013. (128 pages) ISBN 978-3-406-65213-4.
    This book offers an overview of the Gilgamesh epic and its cultural context for the general reader.
