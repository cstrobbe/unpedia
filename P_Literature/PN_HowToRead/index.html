How to Read Literature

You cannot teach a person to read well unless you, and particularly if he has a fine intelligence to begin with, a native aptitude, unless you make him struggle with texts that are very difficult and over his head. (…) The person who is (…) reading, however widely it is, has dealt with readily intelligible stuff, stuff he doesn't have to struggle to master, struggle to understand, is a person whose mind will not be pulled up to its full capacity. (Mortimer J. Adler in an interview with William F. Buckley Jr.).

  • Eagleton, Terry: How to Read Literature. Yale University Press, 2014. (232 pages) ISBN 9780300205305.
    How to Read Literature on Archive.org.
    From the publisher's book description: (…) How to Read Literature is the book of choice for students new to the study of literature and for all other readers interested in deepening their understanding and enriching their reading experience.
    In a series of brilliant analyses, Eagleton shows how to read with due attention to tone, rhythm, texture, syntax, allusion, ambiguity, and other formal aspects of literary works. He also examines broader questions of character, plot, narrative, the creative imagination, the meaning of fictionality, and the tension between what works of literature say and what they show. (…)
    This book is written in an accessible style that should appeal to many readers but probably assumes more knowledge of literature than a beginning student would have. In addition, the book does not really teach students to read literature, since it does not really tell readers what to look for in literary texts, but instead presents a number of Eagleton's own readings—with a strong emphasis on language—and his comments on them.
    See also Jesse Kavadlo's review How to Read Terry Eagleton's 'How to Read Literature' (PopMatters, 22.01.2020).
