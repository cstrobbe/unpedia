This is about exercises that are chosen to that most of the concentration is focused on effort instead of co-ordination. This results in the following types of exercises: (1) an upper-body push in the horizontal plane, (2) an upper-body pull in the horizontal plane, (3) an upper-body push in the vertical plane, (4) an upper-body pull in the vertical plane and (5) a pressing movement with the lower body.     • a lower body push, e.g. leg press or squat,
    • an upper body horizontal pull, e.g. a multi-joint row,
    • an upper body horizontal push, e.g. a chest press or bench press,
    • an upper body vertical pull, e.g. a pulldown or chin-up.

    • Top 10 Scientific Training Tips to Maximize Muscle Growth and to Bring up Weak Body-parts EXPLAINED! (17 minutes, 11.05.2019).
      1. Always warm up with light to moderate cardio. You should be slightly sweating at the start of the training session.
      2. Train the hardest exercises first, instead of pre-fatiguing muscles. For example, if squats are the hardest exercise in the workout, do these first.
      3. Instead of counting reps, time your sets, since “time under tension” is what counts. For muscle hypertrophy, the time under tension should be around 40 seconds; a shorter time under tension benefits strength rather than hypertrophy. But sets should not be shorter than 20 seconds.
      4. The work-to-rest ratio should be between three-to-one and five-to-one. For example, if a set takes one minute (which is long), the rest period should be between three and five minutes. Shorter periods turn the workout into toning or cardio training and force you to use lower weights.
      5. Make the eccentric phase longer than the concentric phase; ideally twice as long. If you do 12 reps that last three seconds each, you get close to 40 seconds of time under tension.
      6. Train hard sets close to failure. (If you can do five sets of 10 reps, the first four sets were too light.)
      7. Use proper form. Otherwise you either don't hit the right muscles, or you get injured.
      8. Active rest helps clearing lactic acid from the muscles.
      9. Train each body part twice a week, especially weak body parts. A small muscle such as the biceps can handle between 15 and 20 sets per week, which means 7 to 10 sets per session. Bigger muscles such as the back and the legs can handle 20 to 30 sets per week. The exact number depends on the person, training experiecen and other factors.
      10. Always stretch after a workout in order to prevent tightness and injuries. (Stretching before a workout makes you weaker.)
    • How To Properly Warm-Up For Your Hard Training Sessions - Weight - Sets - Reps (17 minutes, 05.02.2020).
      This is not about the cardio warmup, which is easy, but about warmups with weights. Warmup sets are meant to prepare you for the actual sets that count towards the workout. Most people need two or three warmup sets before the actual hard sets. In the first warmup should uses roughly half the weight of a hard sets and run to double the number of reps of a hard set. After the warmup set, you should rest until the muscle no longer feels fatigued. The second warmup set should use around 70 percent of the weight of a hard set and consist of fewer reps than the first warmup set. This set should still be easy; you should be able to do ten more reps if you wanted to. After the second warmup set, you will need to rest a bit longer, possibly a minute to a minute and a half. The last warmup set should have a rep range of 10 to 12. If you plan to train to failure with heavey weights and a low rep range, you will need more warmup sets.
      If after the hard sets for the first exercise, you plan to do another exercise for the same muscle, you need only one or two warmup sets because the muscle is already warmed up.

Training Advice

For Beginners

  • Suggestions For Lifting Beginners (Every Damn Day Fitness on YouTube, 6 minutes, 09.05.2018).
    Alan Roberts sells several training programmes on his site, for example E.D.D.F. LEAN MASS HYPERTROPHY PROGRAM and E.D.D.F. VOLUME TRAINING. In this no-nonsense video, he explains that he once considered creating a programme for beginners and why he decided not to do that after all. Beginners should learn the four basic lifts (the bench press, the squat, the deadlift and the overhead press), but these exercises shouldn't be taught using distance learning. The reason is that there are both mental and physical variables that need to be taken into account by a coach who is physically present can correct (and a video can't). Physiological variablese that need to be taken into account include height, weight, age, body fat percentage, the length of the femur, even the length of the femur's neck.
    For this reason, Alan Roberts recommends that beginners should find a strength coach who can teach the four basic lifts in person. After about six months of getting proficient at these exercises, beginners can start worrying about curls or a novice programme, i.e. a three-times-per-week full-body programme that builds on the four basic lifts. Males who have between 12 and 20% body fat shouldn't worry too much about nutrition as long as they stay withing that range. If beginners start aiming for caloric deficits in order to make their abs visible, they will rob themselves of what Alan Roberts calls "noob gains" or lean mass gains. As long as you eat healthy (with sufficient protein, i.e. 1 gram of protein per pound of lean body mass) and work out hard using the four basic lifts, you will develop a good-looking physique. Alan Robets doesn't believe in supplemenation for beginners; they should be getting enough protein through regular food. He stresses the fact that training needs to be consistent: fitness is a lifestyle.

Nutrition and Supplements

Proteins

  • How Much Protein To Build Muscle? The TRUTH ! (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 12 minutes, 16.06.2018).
    According to a review and meta-analysis by Morton et al., protein intakes at amounts greater than ~1.6 g/kg/day do not further contribute RET-induced gains in FFM. People on social media recommend a very wide range of numbers regarding protein intake. For example, Jim Stoppani recommends 1.5–2 grams per pound of bodyweight. Imar Isuf recommends 1 gram per pound of bodyweight and says you don't need to obsess about the perfect amount of good protein in every meal as long as you get enough protein in total. Mike Dolce does not give specific numbers but focuses on good-quality nutrients. Eric Helms recommends 0.8–1.3 grams of protein per pound of bodyweight. Helms also says the amount of protein you need is not as high as people are led to believe.
    James says, How much protein you take depends very heavily on your specific characteristics. These characteristics include the following: (1) your training level and intensity (reps, frequency, etcetera, how much muscle you break down); (2) your body composition; (3) your goals (maintaining muscle mass or increasing it); (4) your current state (or you an athlete or do you exercise of leisure); (5) are you natural or chemically enhanced; (6) your eating protocol.
    Another factor is the thermic effect of food: the digestion of macronutrients also burns calories, and proteins have a higher thermic effect than carbohydrates and fats: as high as 30 % of the calories from protein are burnt up during this process.
    Protein intake recommendations on social media are often inflated. The leaner you get, however, the more you might want to increase the protein intake as you drop and manipulate other macronutrients. It is better to look at your fitness over a long period of time instead of panicking about your protein intake in a single meal.
    References:
    • Morton, Robert W.; Murphy, Kevin T.; McKellar, Sean R.; Schoenfeld, Brad J.; Henselmans, Menno; Helms, Eric; et al: A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength in healthy adults, British Journal of Sports Medicine, March 2018.
    • Morton, Robert W.; Murphy, Kevin T.; et al: Correction: A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength in healthy adults, British Journal of Sports Medicine, September 2020 (CC BY-NC).
    • Helms, Eric R.; et al: A Systematic Review of Dietary Protein During Caloric Restriction in Resistance Trained Lean Athletes: A Case for Higher Intakes, International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism, Volume 24, Issue 2 (2014; paywalled article).
  • Protein Science For Muscle Growth (The Hierarchy of Importance) (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 11 minutes, 09.11.2020).
    This video is for the regular fitness enthusiast who wants to gain muscle. James presents a pyramid to help people take decision about their protein intake. Quality protein means that all nine essential amino acids not to be represented: histidine, isoleucine, leucine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine and lysine. Not all of these need to come from one piece of food; they can also come from a combination of foods such as rice and beans.
    The first layer of the pyramid is the amount of protein you need. There is no consensus on the exact amount of protein intake for muscle growth; instead, we have a range of figures. These recommendations may be expressed in various ways, for example, grams per kilogram of total body weight, grams per kilogram of lean mass or grams per pound of bodyweight. Schoenfeld and Aragon found that 1.6–2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of total body weight is the range of protein needed for growth. Beyond 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of total body weight there seems to be a point of diminishing returns.
    The second layer of the pyramid is the frequency of protein intake. According to Schoenfeld and Aragon, it makes sense to take in 0.4 grams of protein per kilogram of bodyweight per meal across four meals. This can help a state of net anabolism over the day.
    The third and least important layer of the pyramid is the timing of protein intake. There is no post-workout anabolic window. The timing of protein intake is much less important than getting the quantity right. However, if you are fasted before your workout, the need for protein intake after your workout increases. If you have had a pre-workout meal, the need for protein intake after your workout decreases.
    References:
    • Schoenfeld, Brad Jon; Aragon, Alan Albert: How much protein can the body use in a single meal for muscle-building? Implications for daily protein distribution, Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition, 2018 (Open Access).
    • Morton, Robert W.; Murphy, Kevin T.; McKellar, Sean R.; Schoenfeld, Brad J.; Henselmans, Menno; Helms, Eric; et al: A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength in healthy adults, British Journal of Sports Medicine, March 2018.

Supplements

  • MYTHBUSTERS #3: BCAA's The TRUTH ! (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 12 minutes, 11.09.2018).
    Research on branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs) is often misrepresented on social media. There are nine esssential amino acids: histidine, lysine, threonine, isoleucine, leucine, valine, methionine, tryptophan and phenylalanine. Three of these are branched-chain amino acids: leucine, isoleucine and valine. (There are many more proteinogenic amino acid and not all proteins in the body are muscle tissue.) You need the full range of essential amino acids for effective protein synthesis and it is best to take a food-first approach for this instead of relying on supplements. BCAA supplementation in isolation probably leads to suboptimal results compared to consuming protein sources that contain all nine essential amino acids. You probably get all the BCAAs through your food; see Ask The Macro Manager: What Are The Best BCAA Food Sources? by Mike Roussell on Bodybuilding.com (31.07.2018). Vegans may benefit from BCAA supplementation because they don't get enough of them through food alone. However, there is also research that suggests that taking in BCAA in a fasted state may decrease muscle mass (Robert Wolfe, 2017).
  • CREATINE: What Is It and Does It Work ? (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 9 minutes, 27.01.2019).
    Creatine allows the body to create energy by converting adenosine diphosphate (ADP) into adenosine triphosphate (ATP). This may allow you to work harder in the gym and therefore get more results.
    However, there is no direct causal relationship between creatine intake and muscle growth. Muscle growth requires progressive overload, consistency over time, an adequate protein intake and a caloric surplus. Supplements are not hacks that make these requirements optional.
    The body uses ATP for energy, turning it into ADP in the process. The creatine phosphate will gave a phosophate to the ADP to turn it into ATP again. Creatine is naturally available in the body and can be ingested trough food (e.g. meat), but it is difficult for an adult to take in enough creatine through food to get an effective dose (around 3–5 grams per day). (It would require a kilogram of beef, for example.) That does not imply that you need to take a creatine supplement.
    Creatine monohydrate is the most common form and is economical. There is no need to complicate creatine intake. You can even use a loading phase in which you take 20 grams of creatine for a week, which allows faster saturation of creatine in your body. Otherwise, you cant take 3–5 grams per day.
  • AVOID These Supplements (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 12 minutes, 07.04.2019).
    The supplements include glutamine, branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs), L- Carnitine and conjugated linoleic acid (CLA).
    Glutamine has no benefit for muscle growth and recovery but only to people who have suffered extreme injury (who may get it injected). (It is a cheap ingredient that is essentially just a cheap filler in supplements.)
    Branched-chain amino acids are useless. There has been research into BCAAs in rodents, which does not translate very well to humans.
    Fat-loss supplements are also mostly useless, especially in the long run. A caloric deficit is more effective. People who recommend L- Carnitine for fat loss have not properly read the relevant research (e.g. about its effectiveness in overweight cats). CLA has been studied in rodents; there is no evidence that it has a significant effect on fat loss in humans.

Criticism of Misleading or Dangerous Examples and Trends

Social media—YouTube, Facebook, Instagram, Tiktok—are full of influencers who set bad examples by demonstrating exercices that are dangerous in themselves (e.g. weight-lifting exercises while standing on a medicine ball) or because they are executed with bad form (typically because they are performed by people who are not qualified as weightlifting or sports coaches). Other people try to debunk the false information that is spread by incompetent social media influencers.
(YouTubers and influencers that are best avoided include V Shred, Vince Del Monte, Chris Heria (especially when talking about nutrition), Kali Muscle, Jason Blaha and Jim Stoppani.)

  • The Cringe King of Fitness Just Got Worse (Shredded Sports Science on YouTube, 11 minutes, 01.10.2020).
    This video was triggered by Kinobody's pre-workout product advertisement. Pre-workout products are not necessary, just a matter of preference. The decision should not be based on the product's taste or on anecdotal evidence, but on the ingredients. The primary ingredients are clearly show on the packaging, but some ingredients in Kinobody's pre-workout are redundant. Caffeine is an ergogenic aid and is a valid ingredient and 150 mg (per serving of 10.5 grams) is not a megadose. If you do not respond well to caffeine, this product may not be for you. However, it is not significant aid to fat loss in the long term. The claims about how l-theanene works with caffeine are also correct. Thiamine (or vitamin B1) does not need to be supplemented if you have a healthy diet. The product's claims about biotin (or vitamin B7) are not supported by research (except in rats). The relevance of ginseng to fatigue is not supported by evidence, either. l-citrulline might help achieve the “pump”; some products use citrulline malate instead. The claim that l-citrulline increases protein synthesis is not correct; research results on this are contradictory. According to the online article Citrulline Malate vs Citrulline by Robert A. Schinetsky (July 2018), citrulline malate is far more effective in a pre-workout. In conclusion, it is not clear whether this product is more effective than having a pre-workout meal or a cup of coffee.

  • Schoenfeld, Brad; Kolber, Morey; Haimes, Jonathan: The Upright Row: Implications for Preventing Subacromial Impingement, Strength and Conditioning Journal, Volume 33, Issue 5 (October 2011): 25–28.
    In the traditional upright row, the elbows end up above the shoulders and the wrists, which requires internal rotation in the shoulders. The authors propose modifications to the technique to make it safe and effective. The bar should be raised through the elbows, not the wrists, and should move as close to the body as possible. In addition, the arms should be raised so they make an angle of at most 90° with the upper body. The authors also make recommendations regarding training volume, rest periods and weight.
