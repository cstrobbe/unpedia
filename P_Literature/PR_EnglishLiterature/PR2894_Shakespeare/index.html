Shakespeare's Language

  • Magnusson, Lynne; Schalkwyk, David (editors): The Cambridge Companion to Shakespeare's Language. Cambridge Companions to Literature. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2019. (298 pages) ISBN 9781107583184.
    From the publisher's book description:
    The Cambridge Companion to Shakespeare's Language provides important contexts for understanding Shakespeare's experiments with language and offers accessible approaches to engaging with it directly and pleasurably. Incorporating both practical analysis and exemplary readings of Shakespearean passages, it covers elements of style, metre, speech action and dialogue; examines the shaping contexts of rhetorical education and social language; test-drives newly available digital methodologies and technologies; and considers Shakespeare's language in relation to performance, translation and popular culture.

The Tempest

  • Charry, Brinda: The Tempest: Language and Writing. The Arden Shakespeare. Bloomsbury, 2013. (208 pages) ISBN 9781408152898.
  • Singh, Jyotsna: Post-colonial reading of The Tempest, Discovering Literature: Shakespeare & Renaissance (British Library), 15.03.2016.
    This article mentions Aimé Césaire's play Une tempête (1969) and Ngugi Wa Thiong'o's novel A Grain of Wheat (1967) but not Octave Mannoni's book Psychologie de la colonisation (1950) or Stephen Greenblatt's 1976 essay “Learning to curse”.
  • Octave Mannoni: French ethnologue, philosopher and psychoanalyst who was the first to read The Tempest from a colonialist perspective. See his 1950 book Psychologie de la colonisation, reprinted in 1984 as Prospero et Caliban (Éditions Universitaires) and in 1997 as Le racisme revisité (Denoël).

The Authorship Debate

  • Nance, Tim: Who REALLY wrote Shakespeare's plays?!? (Tim Nance on YouTube, 4 minutes, 23.04.2019). This video provides a brief introduction to the so-called controversy.
  • Wikipedia contributors: Shakespeare authorship question, Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia.
  • The Shakespeare Authorship Question (Shakespeare Birthplace Trust on YouTube, 15 minutes, 21.03.2017).
    This video was part of the online course Shakespeare and his World, which was hosted on FutureLearn.
  • Who Wrote Shakespeare? Sir Jonathan Bate & Alexander Waugh (How To Academy, 84 minutes, 26.09.2017).
    This is a video recording that took place at the Emmanuel Centre in London on 21.09.2017. The participants were Jonathan Bate, Professor of English Literature at Oxford University, and Alexander Waugh, chairman of the De Vere Society; the debate was moderated by Hermione Eyre.
  • The Bard debate: Did Shakespeare really write the plays? (Brunel University London on YouTube, 97 minutes, 06.06.2018).
    This is a video recording of a debate that took place at Brunel University London during the Shakespeare week on 23–26 April 2018. The participants are Prof. Alan H. Nelson from the University of Berkeley, California, and Dr. Ros Barber from Goldsmith’s University. The debate is specifically about whether Shakespeare wrote the poems and plays that are attributed to him, not about who else might have written them.
  • Gibson, H. N.: The Shakespeare Claimants: A Critical Survey of the Four Principal Theories concerning the Authorship of the Shakespearean Plays. Routledge, 1952, 2013. (330 pages) ISBN 9780415850551.
  • Shapiro, James: Contested Will: Who Wrote Shakespeare?. Faber & Faber, 2011. ISBN 9780571235773.
  • Edmondson, Paul; Wells, Stanley: Shakespeare beyond Doubt: Evidence, Argument, Controversy. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2013. (298 pages) ISBN 9781107603288.
    From the publisher's description:
    This authoritative, accessible and frequently entertaining book sets the debate in its historical context and provides an account of its main protagonists and their theories. Presenting the authorship of Shakespeare's works in relation to historiography, psychology and literary theory, twenty-three distinguished scholars reposition and develop the discussion. The book explores the issues in the light of biographical, textual and bibliographical evidence to bring fresh perspectives to an intriguing cultural phenomenon.
  • Taylor, Gary; Egan, Gabriel (editors): The New Oxford Shakespeare: Authorship Companion. Oxford University Press, 2017. (776 pages) ISBN 9780199591169.
