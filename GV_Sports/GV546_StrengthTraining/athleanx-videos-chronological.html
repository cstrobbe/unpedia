  20. How To Build Bigger Biceps with the Athlean X Biceps Blast (9 minutes, 16.07.2009).
    In the introduction, Jeff points out that the AthleanX website has been revamped, after which he explains four biceps exercises that he uses as the foundation of his own workouts. He starts with a straight-bar biceps curl and explains that he allows a little bit of a cheat on this exercise, even though he usually stresses correct form. He demonstrates a biceps curl that focuses on the negative, which takes 4–5 seconds. When the biceps get tired, the (minor) cheat on the upward movements allows more reps focusing on the negative movement.
    He then demonstrates a chin-up in which the upward movement does not go all the way up but only to a 90° angle between upper arm and forearm; at the top of the movement, you should hold for a second, then do a slow negative (doing 8–10 reps).
    The third exercise is like a curl but using tubes instead of a bar; since this is a lighter exercise, the tempo is a bit faster and the rep count is between 30 and 40. The advantage of a resistance band compared to a bar is the different strength curve: with a bar, the hardest part is when the forearm is parallel to the floor, whereas with a band, the movement becomes harder as the forearms move to the top of the movement.
    The final exercise is a dead-arm curl: since the easiest position in a regular curl is at the top, this curl exercise is done standing in a baseball fielder position, i.e. bent over, so that the hardest part is at the top of the movement. In this exercise, too, you should focus on the negative.
    Jeff also answered many questions in the comments. In one of his comments, he points out,
    If you're pressed for time, the great way to perform this workout is by alternating biceps and triceps exercises with little to no rest in between sets! Do a set of biceps, immediately move to the rested triceps muscles for a set. By the time you've completed your triceps move, you're biceps have had a chance to recover! You can knock out 9-12 sets of each in less than 20 minutes!
    In another comment, he points out that,
    Biceps are a small muscle group and don't need much volume to stimulate growth! Perform 1 round of each of the exercises (minimizing the rest in between to just enough that allows you to recover for the next exercise)...then rest 2 minutes in between the rounds. Perform another round. Limit it to 2-3 total rounds.
    And again:
    Biceps are a small muscle group and therefore, to prevent overtraining, respond best to 3 sets of 10-12 reps for about 3 exercises. 9 sets total is plenty if you are training them with good intensity. Use an amount of weight that allows you to fail in the given rep range.
    In another comment, he points out that you don't need to train your biceps three times per week; one or twice per week is fine. He also points out that, the biggest factor for biceps size is slowing the negative portion of each exercise. The biceps responds extremely well to slow negatives.

During the lunges with overhead twist, you raise the ball during the lunge and bring it down again while moving back to the standing position. The RDL is a type of lift with the med ball in which one leg is extended backward in the lower part of the movement. In the curl catches, you throw up the med ball and catch it while slowing down the downward movement.

  32. 1 Tip To More Muscle - Find Out How Long Your Workout Should Be (4 minutes, 16.11.2009).
    If you want to build muscle, you need a “time under tension” of about 45–60 seconds. If each rep lasted four seconds and you did 12 reps, your muscles would be under tension for 48 seconds; that is the origin of the magic number 12. Tension exists both in the positive and the negative portion of a repetition; for example, when doing pullups, you could do the reps 2 seconds up and 2 seconds down, or 1 second up and 3 seconds down. However, a negative movement of three seconds is more challenging. If you spend only 20 seconds on a set, as many people do, you miss opportunities for building muscle.
    See also Jeff Cavaliere's blog post Maximum Muscle Building - When 8 Reps Becomes the New 12 Reps.

  1. How to Get Jacked Forearms (CONTROVERSIAL!) (9 minutes, 01.03.2020).
    Just like the triceps contributes a lot to the volume of the upper arm, the brachioradialis contributes a lot to the volume of the forearm. Jeff uses his muscle markers to show where the brachioradialis attaches to the humerus and the radius. Unlike true forearm muscles, the brachioradialis has no action on the wrist.
    This leads to the question in what position the forearm should be in order to train this muscle: supinated, neutral or pronated? In a supinated position, the biceps does the work of flexing the elbow. In a neutral grip position the brachioradialis gets also activated. However, the brachioradialis can also pronate the forearm, so in order to train the muscle, we need both pronation and flexion.
    The first exercise you can do is reverse barbell curls, using either a straight bar with a lower weight (fully pronated position of the forearm) or an EZ bar, which brings the wrists a bit closer to a neutral position, which also allows you to use more weight.
    The second exercise you can do is the reverse lat pull down (towards and past the head). Instead of the bar, you can also use the ropes you otherwise use for face pulls.
    The third exercise you can do is battle ropes using a neutral or a pronated grip.
    The fourth exercise you can do is biceps curls in which you grip the dumbbells close to the plates in the middle of the body and then curl while at the same time turning the forearm into a pronated position.
    Finally, putting plates back after you're done with an exercise also activates the brachioradialis and is good gym etiquette.
