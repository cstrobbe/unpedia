Many articles and blog posts point out that Gulliver's Travels is not a children's book, but they usually don't explain when people started treating it as such:

  • Cook, Daniel: Gulliver’s Travels Wasn’t Meant to Be a Children’s Book And More Things You Didn’t Know About the Literary Classic, Smithsonian, 28.11.2017.
  • Allen, Sally: In which I read Gulliver’s Travels and am vexed, SallyAllenBooks.com, 22.01.2017.
  • Escobedo, Silvia: Was Gulliver’s Travels meant to be a children’s book?, Grupo Tierra Trivium, 18.03.2018.
  • Stör, Christian: Gullivers Reisen, Die neue Bücherkiste (no date).
  • The Adventures of Captain Gulliver, The British Library (no date).
    Page about an item in the British Library's collection: a pocket-sized edition of Gulliver's Travels abridged and adapted for children. It omits Parts 3 and 4 of the novel.
  • Nicole: Curator's Choice: Gulliver's Travels, Toronto Reference Library Blog, 21.04.2016.
    According to this blog post, the book appealed to children from its very beginnings and Francis Newbery published an abridged edition for children in 1776: The Adventures of Captain Gulliver in a Voyage to the Islands of Lilliput and Brogdingnag — Abridged from the Works of The Celebrated Dean Swift. (According to Vera Tweddell, this appears to be the first "juvenile" edition of Gulliver and a very rare book.)
