Literature Stack Exchange Reading Challenges

Background on Reading Challenges

The monthly topic challenges are a result of the feedback on the question Do we want something akin to topic challenges for books outside our bailiwick, to help alleviate the book diversity problem? The outcome was overwhelmingly positive. Some other sites run weekly topic challenges, as described on Meta Stack Exchange: How do weekly topic challenges work? However, due to the time it takes to read a book (added to the time it takes to borrow it from a library or to buy it), weekly topic challenges would be too short. Suggest your Lit.SE reading challenges here! is the old meta post where participants can submit new proposals for monthly challenges. Monthly Topic Challenges aren't getting much attention. How can we change them? is a meta post from July 2019 asking for proposals to increase contributions to the monthly challenges. As a result of this, the reading challenges were changed (see below) and the meta post New Literature SE Topic Challenge Suggestions Thread was created in April 2020 to replace the old meta post.

There were no reading challenges in December 2017, January 2018, August 2019, September 2019 and December 2019. For each topic challenge, the number of questions, answers, views, participants and votes refer to the activity during the topic challenge. Answers, votes, views etcetera that were submitted later don't count, even though it would be interesting to see which topic remained “popular” after the topic challenge.

There were no reading challenges in August and September 2019. Due to low participation in the preceding months, the reading challenges were revised based on a proposal that each reading challenge would run for two months, that the next reading challenge would be announced one month in advance and that a new challenge would be started each month, so there would always be two reading challenges running concurrently.

Potential Suggestions for Future Reading Challenges

In languages other than English:

  • Clarice Lispector (1920 – 1977), a Brazilian novelist and short story writer acclaimed internationally for her innovative novels and short stories.
  • Joaquim Maria Machado de Assis (1839 – 1908), a pioneer Brazilian novelist, poet, playwright and short story writer, widely regarded as the greatest writer of Brazilian literature.
  • Irène Némirovsky (1903 – 1942), best known for Suite française, published posthumously in 2004.
  • Ales Adamovich (1927 – 1994), Belarusian Soviet writer and a critic, best known for The Khatyn Story and The Blockade Book.
  • Imre Kertész (1929 – 2016), Hungarian author, awarded Nobel Prize in Literature in 2002.
  • Vicki Baum (1888 – 1960), best known for Menschen im Hotel (People at a Hotel / Grand Hotel, 1929).
  • Das kunstseidene Mädchen (1932) by Irmgard Keun; apparently not translated into English.
  • Witold Gombrowicz (1904 – 1969).
  • Danilo Kiš (1935 – 1989), Yugoslav novelist, short story writer, essayist and translator.
  • Leonardo Sciascia (1921 – 1989), Italian author (hard to find in translation?).
  • Daniil Kharms (1905 – 1942), early Soviet-era avant-gardist and absurdist poet, writer and dramatist. See also 10 Offbeat Literary Works of Non-English Writers (Listverse, 2012).
  • Mariama Bâ (1929 – 1981), Senegalese author and feminist.
  • Christine de Pizan (1364 – c. 1430), poet and author at the court of King Charles VI of France and several French dukes.
  • Subodh Ghosh (1909–1980), Indian author of Bengali literature.
  • Women Writers of Ancient Greece and Rome: An Anthology, edited by Ian Plant. Equinox Publishing, 2004. (276 pages; ISBN 9781904768029)
  • Adile Sultan's Divan by Adile Sultan (1826 – 1899), an Ottoman princess.
  • Annemarie Schwarzenbach (1908 – 1942), Swiss writer and journalist. She wrote in German and most of her works have not been translated into English.
  • Benito Pérez Galdós (1843 – 1920), Spanish realist novelist; some scholars consider him second only to Miguel de Cervantes in stature as a Spanish novelist.
  • Babur: The Babur Nama. Translated by Annette Susannah Beveridge. Introduction by William Dalrymple. Everyman's Library Classics Series. November 2020. (1032 pages) ISBN 9781101908235.
  • Ibn Fadlan: Ibn Fadlan and the Land of Darkness: Arab Travellers in the Far North. Translated by Caroline Stone and Paul Lunde. Penguin, 2011. (304 pages) ISBN 9780140455076.
  • Walters, Joseph Jeffrey: Guanya Pau: A Story of an African Princess. Broadview Press, 2004. (205 pages) ISBN 9781551113654.
    From the publisher's book description: The first book of long fiction by an African to be published in English, this novel tells the story of a young woman of the Vai people in Liberia. Guanya Pau, betrothed as a child to a much older, polygamous man, flees her home rather than be forced into marriage, and the novel recounts her subsequent efforts to reach the Christian community where the man she loves awaits her. Joseph Jeffrey Walters was a Vai man who converted to Christianity, and this, his only novel, is a remarkably complex work, embracing both Christian beliefs and a deep pride in his African heritage.
  • Maurice Maeterlinck (1862 – 1949): Flemish author who wrote in French, and so far the only Belgian author who was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature. Best known for Pelléas et Mélisande and L'Oiseau bleu.
  • Laurent Binet (born in 1972).

In English:

  • Aphra Behn (1640 – 1689), one of the first English women to earn her living by her writing.
  • T. J. Bass, author of the science fiction novels Half Past Human (1971) and The Godwhale (1974).
  • James Baldwin (1924 – 1987), author of the essay collection Notes of a Native Son (1955), Go Tell It on the Mountain and Giovanni's Room.
  • Dorothea Fairbridge (1862 – 1931), South African author.
  • Olive Schreiner (1855 – 1920), South African author, anti-war campaigner and intellectual.
  • Works of literature whose title contains the word cheese or the name of a specific type of cheese, for example, I Am the Cheese (1977) by Robert Cormier, Processed Cheese, a novel by Stephen Wright, The Great American Cheese War, a comedy thriller by Paul Flower, To Brie or Not To Brie, a "cheese shop mystery" by Avery Aames.

Non-fiction:

  • Alexandra David-Néel (1868 – 1969), Belgian–French explorer and writer who wrote over 30 books about Eastern religion, philosophy and her travels. (She traveled to India, Tibet, Nepal, China, Korea, Japan and Mongolia. She visited Lhasa in 1924, when it was still forbidden to foreigners.)
  • The Memoirs of Lady Hyegyeong by Lady Hyegyeong (1735 – 1816).

Already suggested:

  • Jorge Amado (1912 – 2001): Brazilian modernist writer whose work contains references to Candomblé religion. Suggestion for Jorge Amado
  • Chinghiz Aitmatov (1928 – 2008), Soviet and Kyrgyz author.
