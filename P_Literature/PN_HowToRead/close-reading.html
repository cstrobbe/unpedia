Close Reading

Lira bien qui lira le dernier. (Gérard Genette, Palimpsestes. La littérature au second degré).

What Is Close Reading?

Close reading is an approach to analysing a text that pays careful attention to the text as such, rather than aspects such as historical, literary or biographical context. It is concerned with both content and form, and focuses on aspects such as word choice, register, syntax, rhyme scheme, metre, figurative language and tone. One might say that this type of reading pays meticulous attention to the “literariness” of a text, but, strictly speaking, close reading can also be applied to non-fiction. This type of analysis is thorough and time-consuming, which is why it is typically applied to relatively short texts or passages. For example, a close reading of a single sonnet can run to several pages.

Books About Close Reading

  • Greenham, David: Close Reading: The Basics. Routledge, 2018. (180 pages) ISBN 9781138562226.
  • Federico, Annette: Engagements with Close Reading. Routledge, 2015. (202 pages) ISBN 9780415748025.
  • Howe, Elizabeth S.: Close Reading: An Introduction to Literature. Pearson, 2010. (320 pages) ISBN 9780132436564.
  • Lentricchia, Frank; DuBois, Andrew (editors): Close Reading: The Reader. Duke University Press, 2002. (408 pages) ISBN 978-0-8223-3039-4.
  • Booth, Stephen: Close Reading without Readings: Essays on Shakespeare and Others. Rowman & Littlefield, 2015. (208 pages) ISBN 978-1-61147-892-1.
    From the publisher's book description: Dealing mainly with the works of William Shakespeare, the essays in Close Readings without Readings reflect Stephen Booth’s lifelong interest in uncovering the ways great literature works upon readers. As the book’s title suggests, the author does not aim to create new or novel interpretations or to uncover the political agendas of literary works, but to notice language patterns—repetitions, analogies, correspondences, echoes, overtones—and other ways in which the choice and the arrangement of words affect readers. For Booth, close reading is a practice of attentiveness. He notices how, why, and in what ways Shakespeare’s works affect his readers. (…)
  • James, David (editor): Modernism and Close Reading. Oxford University Press, 2020. (272 pages) ISBN 9780198749967.
  • Eve, Martin Paul: Close Reading with Computers: Textual Scholarship, Computational Formalism, and David Mitchell's Cloud Atlas. Stanford University Press, 2019. (272 pages) ISBN 9781503609365.
    From the publisher's book description: Most contemporary digital studies are interested in distant-reading paradigms for large-scale literary history. This book asks what happens when such telescopic techniques function as a microscope instead. The first monograph to bring a range of computational methods to bear on a single novel in a sustained fashion, it focuses on the award-winning and genre-bending Cloud Atlas (2004). (…) Martin Paul Eve demonstrates a set of methods and provides open-source software tools that others can use in their own literary-critical practices. In this way, the project serves as a bridge between users of digital methods and those engaged in more traditional literary-critical endeavors.

Endorsing the concept of “autotelic text”, that a text is a unified entity, complete in itself, and containing meaning without any reference to external evidence such as the author’s intention/history, biography or the socio-cultural conditions of its production, the New Critics, Wimsatt and Beardsley cautioned against the fallacies of judging a literary work based on the author’s intention or its impression on the reader, what they called “intentional Fallacy” and “Affective Fallacy”.
