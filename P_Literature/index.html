Literary Merit

  • Konchar Farr, Cecilia: The Ulysses Delusion: Rethinking Standards of Literary Merit. American Literature Readings in the 21st Century. Palgrave Macmillan, 2016. (XX + 219 pages) ISBN 978-1-349-71647-0.
    Publisher's book description: Popular fiction follows literature professors wherever they go. At coffee shops or out for drinks, after faculty meetings or classes, even at family reunions – they are persistently pressed to talk about bestselling novels. Questions immediately follow: What do I mean when I say a book is "good"? Why do contemporary novels like these, conversations like these, matter to professors of literature? Shouldn't they be spending their time re-reading The Great Gatsby? The Ulysses Delusion confronts these questions and answers their call for more engaged conversations about books. Through topics like the Oprah's Book Club, Harry Potter, and Chick Lit, Cecilia Konchar Farr explores the lively, democratic, and gendered history of novels in the US. as a context for understanding how avid readers and literary professionals have come to assess them so differently.
  • Hartmann, Cristina: How to Tell if a Piece of Fiction Has Literary Merit, Medium, 21.06.2017 (7-minute read).
    This blog post breaks literary merit down into three categories of characteristics: storytelling, style and historical significance. Storytelling can be broken down into the following four aspects: characters, plot, world-building and themes. Style can be broken down into three aspects: prose, dialogue and narrative. Hartmann breaks down historical significance into the following aspects: time period or region (does a work of fiction define our understanding of a particular time period or place?), underrepresented perspective and revolutionary style. Hartmann adds that literary merit should not be confused with taste, and concludes: Literary merit is far less subjective than taste. It’s just that we don’t always care about the stuff that makes a book particularly meritious.
    An earlier version of this blog post was submitted on Quora on 5 October 2015: How can you tell if a piece of fiction has literary merit?
  • Russ, Joanna: Towards an Aesthetic of Science Fiction, Science Fiction Studies, July 1975.
  • What Exactly Is Literary Merit And How Do You Discern It From A Text?, AskLiteraryStudies on Reddit, 25.06.2017.
  • Literary merit vs. entertainment value, Science Fiction & Fantasy forums, December 2013.
  • Is Stephen King meritorious?, AskLiteraryStudies on Reddit, November 2014.
  • Hunter, Lynette: What Is Literary Value?, Gresham College, 15.10.1997.
    Quotes: Throughout the recorded history of writings about literature, oratory, story-telling, or the verbal arts in general, it has never been the case that people have found a specific answer. If I were to ask you to write down on a piece of paper the four most important writers in your life, you would probably want first to know what the list was for- education? Revelations about your inner life? Representing your culture to another’s?
    Hunter also writes that the canon defined by Matthew Arnold's anthology of literature in the 1860s and 1870s and revised by F. R. Leavis, T. S. Eliot and others (in the 1920s) was still largely in place in the late 1990s.
  • Serbu, Damian: The Literary Merits of Speculative Fiction, Lambda Literary, 27.04.2014.
    The author, who has written fiction involving vampires, introduces his text as an opinion piece [that] is primarily a plea with everyone to stop the snotty, highbrow judgments against speculative fiction.
    Quote: In fact, all fiction at its heart and regardless of genre seeks to strip away reality and take readers into new realms. He claims that removing the constraints of reality, speculative fiction dives deeper into the soul. He also claims that markets and libraries create boundaries between speculative fiction and other types of fiction and that these boundaries separate and discriminate.

    The original manuscript of Darkness at Noon was written in German in the south of France in 1939 and finished in Paris in 1940. Until a few years ago, the only known copy was a translation into English by Koestler's 21-year-old girlfriend Daphne Hardy, which was sent to the publisher Jonathan Cape in London. Koestler sent his carbon copy to a publisher in Switzerland, after which the couple Paris at the arrival of German troops. In 2015, Matthias Wessel found Koestler's German manuscript in the archive of Emil Oprecht, founder of the Europa publishing house in Zurich. Based on this original, Vintage has published a new English translation by Philip Boehm.
