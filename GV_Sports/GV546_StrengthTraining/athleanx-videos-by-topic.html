Arms

  1. How to Get Jacked Forearms (CONTROVERSIAL!) (9 minutes, 01.03.2020).
    Just like the triceps contributes a lot to the volume of the upper arm, the brachioradialis contributes a lot to the volume of the forearm. Jeff uses his muscle markers to show where the brachioradialis attaches to the humerus and the radius. Unlike true forearm muscles, the brachioradialis has no action on the wrist.
    This leads to the question in what position the forearm should be in order to train this muscle: supinated, neutral or pronated? In a supinated position, the biceps does the work of flexing the elbow. In a neutral grip position the brachioradialis gets also activated. However, the brachioradialis can also pronate the forearm, so in order to train the muscle, we need both pronation and flexion.
    The first exercise you can do is reverse barbell curls, using either a straight bar with a lower weight (fully pronated position of the forearm) or an EZ bar, which brings the wrists a bit closer to a neutral position, which also allows you to use more weight.
    The second exercise you can do is the reverse lat pull down (towards and past the head). Instead of the bar, you can also use the ropes you otherwise use for face pulls.
    The third exercise you can do is battle ropes using a neutral or a pronated grip.
    The fourth exercise you can do is biceps curls in which you grip the dumbbells close to the plates in the middle of the body and then curl while at the same time turning the forearm into a pronated position.
    Finally, putting plates back after you're done with an exercise also activates the brachioradialis and is good gym etiquette.

Recovery and Avoiding Losing Gains

  • Don’t Ignore THIS Early Warning Sign - MUSCLE LOSS!! (7 minutes, 17.07.2019)
    Grip strength is neuromuscular driven. A loss of grip strength can be a sign that you are overtraining or under-recovering. You can test grip strength using a special device, a hand grip dynamometer, which is rather expensive. Instead, you can also use a bodyweight scale to get a good approximation of what the dynamometer would say. If you notice a signification drop in grip strength, i.e. a 10% drop, you need to change something in your training, such as reducing the volume or taking a deload week.
