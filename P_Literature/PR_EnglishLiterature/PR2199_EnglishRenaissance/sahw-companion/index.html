A Companion to “Shakespeare and His World”

Introduction

This collection of web resources serves as a “companion” to the MOOC Shakespeare and His World. The course was created by Professor Jonathan Bate (Warwick University) in co-operation with the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust. Jonathan Bate is the MOOC's lead educator, and he is sometimes joined by Jennifer Reid of the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust.

The course ran for the first time in March–May 2014 and had around 11,000 registered learners (see Join us in celebrating 450 years of Shakespeare, Warwick Business School, 02 September 2014). The MOOC had its fifth run on FutureLearn from 20 March till 27 May 2017; some of the participants are taking the course for the fifth time, for the quality of the course content itself, the lively and interesting comments or both. At the time of writing, it is not clear whether the course will be repeated.

The MOOC is a 10-week course, with each week focusing on a specific theme. Each week is subdivided in “steps”: one or two introductory steps; six steps with a video that presents an object, usually from the collections of the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust; an assessment that consists of 12 multiple-choice questions; a discussion step where learners can comment on their favourite object of the week; and a summary step with a video in which Jonathan Bate and Jennifer Reid discuss certain themes and ideas from the week. Except for the assessments, each step has a comments section. The number of comments in each step varies between just a few dozen and several hundreds. Many of these comments contain links to other interesting resources. They also contain enough book recommendations to break anyone's book-buying budget.
You can see the first step of the first week (without the comments, which are not public) on the FutureLearn website. To get more impressions of the course content, you can watch the following videos on YouTube. (Some of these videos are only excerpts of videos used in the course.)

  • Shakespeare and his World - MOOC (Warwick Business School) (2 minutes)
  • The Shakespeare Birthplace Trust and Collections (FutureLearn) (5 minutes)
  • A look at William Shakespeare's finances (FutureLearn) (1 minute)
  • William Shakespeare's baptismal records (FutureLearn) (2 minutes)
  • A single page from an early edition of Shakespeare's 'Venus and Adonis' (FutureLearn) (1.5 minutes)
  • What was Shakespeare's first experience of theatre? (FutureLearn) (2 minutes)

Weeks 2 to 9 have a focus play, and the objects presented in the videos are used to illustrate certain excerpts and themes from the plays. It is a good idea, though not a requirement, to read the plays before the start of the week. For those learners who don't have the time to read the entire plays, Jonathan Bate provides a list of four or five scenes that are important to read in the context of the course.

Shakespeare's plays can be read online, for example on the following websites:

  • Open Source Shakespeare, a website that does not only contain most of Shakespeare's works but also advanced search functions and a concordance. This is the electronic edition recommended by Jonathan Bate.
  • Plays and Poems on the website “Internet Shakespeare Editions” hosted at the University of Victoria in Canada and possibly the oldest Shakespeare website that is still online.
  • Shakespeare's Words: Works: the texts on this website are those from the editions published by Penguin Books (except for Cymbeline and Edward III). These texts have annotations in the margins, which is helpful for reading the plays online.
  • The Complete Works of William Shakespeare, a website hosting the digital Complete Moby(tm) Shakespeare. (See How Moby Shakespeare Took Over the Internet for more background about this edition.)
  • The Oxford Shakespeare, a digitised version of the edition prepared by W. J. Craig and originally published in 1914 (and therefore outdated).

However, since Shakespeare's works contain many words, expressions and allusions that are obscure to most modern readers, it is a good idea to read a thoroughly annotated edition of each play. There is a wide choice of annotated editions, for example (in no particular order):

  • The Arden Shakespeare, currently in the hands of Bloomsbury Group;
  • The New Cambridge Shakespeare, published by Cambridge University Press;
  • The Oxford Shakespeare, published by Oxford University Press;
  • The New Penguin Shakespeare, published by Penguin;
  • The RSC Shakespeare, published by Palgrave Macmillan (see also the RSC shop);
  • The Folger Shakespeare Library Editions, published by the Folger Shakespeare Library;
  • the Shakespeare plays in the series Norton Critical Editions, published by W. W. Norton. Not all Shakespeare plays are available in this series. It has editions of the following focus plays: The Merchant of Venice, Othello, Macbeth, Antony and Cleopatra, The Tempest, but not The Merry Wives of Windsor, A Midsummer Night's Dream and Henry V.
  • The Shakespeare plays published as Collins Classics. (The text corresponds to the edition by Peter Alexander, originally published by Collins in 1951: William Shakespeare: The Complete Works.)

Which edition you choose depends on how seriously you want to study Shakespeare, how much convenience you want and how much money you want to spend on books. The New Penguin Shakespeare is relatively cheap, but the notes are at the end of the book instead of the bottom of the page. The Arden, Cambridge, Oxford, RSC, Folger and Norton editions have notes at the bottom of the page. These editions generally also have more extensive introductions, including explanations about the origin of the text and the editorial procedures. So the convenience of having notes on the same page as the text comes brings additional materials that would mainly interest serious students and scholars. The Norton Critical Editions contain an annotated text of the play, a section with comments by actors, a section with extracts from sources used by Shakespeare, a selection of criticism, and sometimes also excerpts from later works that were inspired by the play.

The Shakespeare editions in the Collins Classics series sometimes have a glossary at the end of the volume (e.g. Othello, The Merchant of Venice, Hamlet, King Lear, Julius Caesar, As You Like It and The Taming of the Shrew, ), while other plays have notes pages facing the text (e.g. Henry V, A Midsummer Night's Dream, Macbeth, Much Ado About Nothing, Twelfth Night and Romeo and Juliet).

It can also be helpful to consult a glossary with word definitions that have changed since Shakespeare's time. Several glossaries are available, both online and in print:

  • Crystal, David; Crystal, Ben: Shakespeare's Words: A Glossary and Language Companion. Penguin, 2002. (650 pages) ISBN 0 14 100737 0.
    The book also has a website: Shakespeare's Words.
  • Onions, C. T.; Eagleson, Robert D. (revised edition): A Shakespeare Glossary. Third edition. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1986. ISBN 9780198125211.
  • The Shakespeare Glossary on the website Shakespeare Online.

Open Source Shakespeare also has a Concordance of Shakespeare's complete works.

The Authorship Question

There are a number of persistent and far-fetched theories claiming that William Shakespeare from Stratford-upon-Avon is not the author of the plays attributed to him.

Some relevant links and books:

  • The Shakespeare Authorship Question: a 15-minute video from the course that is available on YouTube.
  • Shakespeare authorship question, History of the Shakespeare authorship question and List of Shakespeare authorship candidates on Wikipedia. The last article lists over 70 candidates, including people who died before Shakespeare stopped writing plays and people who were born of Shakespeare's death.
  • Shakespeare sues John Addenbrooke: writ to bring Addenbrooke’s surety, Thomas Hornby to court, following Addenbrooke’s failure to give satisfaction on the website Shakespeare Documented. This document from June 1609 connects William Shakespeare, gentleman, lately in the court of the lord James, now king of England (in other words, the playwright) with the William Shakespeare from Stratford-upon-Avon. This is one of the documents that supports the case that William Shakespeare from Stratford wrote the plays that have always been contributed to him.
  • Mortgage for the Blackfriars Gatehouse, signed by Shakespeare on the website Shakespeare Documented (already mentioned above). When Shakespeare bought the Blackfriars Gatehouse in London in March 1613, one of his trustees was John Heminge (also known as John Heminges), one of the actors in the King's Men.
  • Conveyance by John and Mary Shakespeare to Robert Webbe, for £4, of their share in two messuages in Snitterfield on the website Shakespeare Documented. (Already mentioned above because of the seals.)
  • Bust of Shakespeare: a plaster cast made in 1814, on the website of the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust.

Playwright and Poet; Acting and Writing

William Shakespeare became a member of the Lord Chamberlain's Men and a shareholder in the Globe theatre. One of the other shareholders was the famous actor Richard Burbage (1567 – 1619).

  • Richard Burbage: oil painting by Charles Fullwood based on an original in the Dulwich Gallery.
  • Stanley Wells: The First Great Shakespearian Actor, Blogging Shakespeare, 26.05.2011.
  • An anecdote in the diary of John Manningham, a student in Middle Temple (13 March 1602) (Wikipedia):

    Upon a time when Burbage played Richard the Third there was a citizen grew so far in liking with him, that before she went from the play she appointed him to come that night unto her by the name of Richard the Third. Shakespeare, overhearing their conclusion, went before, was entertained and at his game ere Burbage came. Then, message being brought that Richard the Third was at the door, Shakespeare caused return to be made that William the Conqueror was before Richard the Third.

Shakespeare's Latin (and Greek)

In Act IV, Scene 1 of The Merry Wives of Windsor, William (William Page, not William Shakespeare) has his Latin tested by the Welsh parson Hugh Evans. The grammar book he would have learnt his Latin from was William Lily's grammar, which had been commissioned in the early 16th century and which remained in use for several centuries.

  • Lily, William: Lily's Grammar of Latin in English: An Introduction of the Eyght Partes of Speche, and the Construction of the Same. Edited and Introduced by Hedwig Gwosdek. Oxford University Press, 2013. (368 pages) ISBN 9780199668113.
    A digitised version of an 1841 reprint, Latinæ grammaticæ rudimenta; or An introduction to the Latin tongue, is available in the Internet Archive.
  • Wrightson, Keith E.: Education and Literacy: lecture that is part of the YaleCourse Early Modern England.
  • Baldwin, Thomas Whitfield: William Shakespere's small Latine & lesse Greeke. Urbana: University of Illinois Press, 1944.
    This is a detailed study in two volumes (1500 pages) of the curricula in Elizabethan grammar schools. See also William Shakspere's Small Latine & Lesse Greeke on the University of Illinois Press website.
  • Burrow, Colin: Shakespeare and Classical Antiquity. Oxford University Press, 2013. (304 pages) ISBN 9780199684793.
    See also Timothy Burns' review in Claremont Review of Books, Vol. XV, Number 1, Winter 2014/15.

Humour-ous Intermezzo: Antonio's Sadness

In the first scene of The Merchant of Venice, Antonio explains that he is said and that he does not know why. His friends try to find an explanation, but he finally says:

I hold the world but as the world, Gratiano;
A stage where every man must play a part,
And mine a sad one.

The Merchant of Venice, Act I, scene 1, lines 82–84.

Some scholars have tried to explain Antonio's melancholy temperament as a consequence of homosexuality (towards Bassanio, who will leave Venice to woo Portia), which could not be expressed in the play. It can also be seen as serving a theatrical purpose, namely to prepare the audience for Antonio's apathetic attitude in later scenes, especially during the trial scene without the need to make the character evolve psychologically during the play.

Is Antonio melancholy in the sense of the theory of the four temperament or humours that Shakespeare often alludes to in his plays? Shakespeare does not make this explicit. For more about the four humours in Elizabethan literature, see

  • Morris, Sylvia: Sadness and the four humours in Shakespeare, The Shakespeare Blog, 18 February 2014.
  • Paster, Gail Kern: William Shakespeare and the Four Humours: Elizabethan Medical Beliefs, YouTube video of a 55-minute lecture given at the University of Pittsburgh on 2 October 2014.
  • Paster, Gail Kern; Rowe, Katherine; Floyd-Wilson, Mary (editors): Reading the Early Modern Passions: Essays in the Cultural History of Emotion. University of Pennsylvania Press, 2004. (392 pages) ISBN 9780812218725.
  • Paster, Gail Kern: Humoring the Body: Emotions and the Shakespearean Stage. University of Chicago Press, 2010 (2014). (290 pages) ISBN 9780226213828.

Shakespeare and Montaigne

One of Shakespeare's sources for The Tempest is the Essays that the French philosopher Michel de Montaigne (1533 – 1592) had started publishing in 1580. John Florio's English translation of the Essays was published in 1603. There is a similarity in wording between a speech by Gonzalo in Act II, Scene 1 of The Tempest and a passage in the essay Of Cannibals.

Music

Caliban's appreciation of the island's music leads to the question of the importance and status of music in Shakespeare's time. The Englishman John Case, who died in 1600, is known as a writer on Aristotle, but was also an authority on music. One of his books is The Praise of Musicke (published in 1586), of which the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust owns a copy. Its full title is The praise of musicke: Wherein besides the antiquitie, dignitie, delectation, & vse thereof in ciuill matters, is also declared the sober and lawfull vse of the same in the congregation and Church of God. See also:

  • Did Music Matter in Shakespeare’s Time? on the website Finding Shakespeare, 17 December 2012.
  • Play On! Shakespeare and Music: an online exhibition on the website Finding Shakespeare.
  • Musica Transalpina: a collection of madrigals published in England in 1588. The music was mainly Italian but the lyrics were in English. This publication inspired many English madrigal composers.
  • The Parley of Instruments: Music for Shakespeare's Company: some excerpts from an album published by Hyperion Records.
  • Jennifer Kampani and Voice of Music: It was a lover and his lass: performance of Thomas Morley's song (1600), which has almost the same lyrics as the song in Shakespeare's As You Like It.

Prospero and Magic

Prospero is represented as a powerful sorcerer or magician. In Act 1, Scene 2, Prospero speaks the following words about his reading:

(…), and for the liberal arts
Without a parallel; those being all my study,
The government I cast upon my brother
And to my state grew stranger, being transported
And rapt in secret studies.

(Quoted from Open Source Shakespeare)

The magic may have reminded the audience of John Dee (1527 – 1608 / 1609), who immersed himself both in sciences and in activities such magic, astrology and occult philosophy. However, there were similar figures, both literary and historical:

  • In literature, the most famous “magician” was probably Faustus in Christopher Marlowe's play Doctor Faustus.
  • Less well known is the magician Peter Fabell in the play The Merry Devil of Edmonton. The play was published in 1608 and probably written around 1600–1604. It was at one point attributed to Shakespeare. (Peter Fabell may have been a historical figure.)
  • The Italian Giambattista della Porta (1535?–1615) was a polymath who wrote plays and studied occult philosophy, astrology, alchemy, mathematics, meteorology and natural philosophy. One of this books was Magia Naturalis, i.e. Natural Magic, translated into English in 1658. (See also Giambattista della Porta in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.)
  • Magic, illusion and the supernatural, The British Library — Discovering Literature: Shakespeare and Renaissance writers: a set of articles on magic and the supernatural in Renaissance plays such as Doctor Faustus, A Midsummer Night's Dream, The Tempest, Macbeth and Ben Jonson's The Alchemist.

  • In step 9.5 (Shakespeare and Montaigne) or in step 9.9 (Caliban the Savage Barbarian), it would be worth adding that in Shakespeare's time “savage” didn't have the current meaning of “cruel, vicious, hostile or bestial [person]” but “wild and uncivilised [person]”.
